# How to Run a Discovery Interview

<span class="part-label">Part 1 — Discovery Craft</span>

::: tip When this chapter is for
You have a meeting with a real user — a vet, a receptionist, a warehouse manager — and you need to leave that meeting understanding their actual experience, not their wishlist. You have thirty to sixty minutes to surface truths that the person themselves may not have articulated before.
:::

## The Direct Answer

- Ask the person to walk you through a specific recent day. "Walk me through last Tuesday" beats "tell me about your workflow" every time.
- When they describe a solution ("I need a button that..."), redirect to the feeling: "What's happening right before you reach for that button?"
- Use silence. When you feel the urge to fill a gap, wait. The next thing they say is almost always more honest than the first.
- Capture the surprises — the moments when the interviewee says something that surprises *them*. Those are the insights.
- Debrief within 30 minutes. After that, you remember the narrative. You lose the texture.

## Why This Works

People don't describe their problems. They describe their solutions to their problems. If you ask Ronit "what do you need from the system?" she will describe a feature — a better search, a faster screen, a notification. What she won't describe is the seven minutes before the first patient arrives, the feeling of not knowing what she is walking into, the three places she checks because no single place has what she needs. The feature request is a symptom. The seven minutes is the diagnosis.

Anchoring in a specific day — not "a typical day" but "last Tuesday" — forces concrete detail. Ronit cannot say "I usually check the schedule" when you ask about Tuesday. She has to say "I opened the notebook, saw fourteen appointments, didn't recognize the name on the third one, and spent four minutes looking for the file." That specificity is where insight lives. Generalities are polished. Specifics are true.

The three question types map to different layers of understanding. Context questions ("what happens before this?") reveal workflow. Feeling questions ("what's the feeling at 8:58?") reveal pain. Surprise questions ("what did you expect to happen vs. what actually happened?") reveal broken assumptions — yours and theirs. You need all three layers. Most interviewers only use the first.

The interview is not a survey. You are not collecting answers to predetermined questions. You are following a person through their day and noticing where they hesitate, where they sigh, where they speed up because they don't want to dwell on something painful. The best discovery interviews feel like conversations, not interrogations. The interviewee should talk 80% of the time. If you are talking more than that, you are performing expertise instead of collecting evidence.

## Step by Step

1. **Before the interview: prepare, but hold loosely.** Write 5-7 questions. Put them on paper beside the keyboard. Know that you will use maybe three of them. The rest of the interview will be follow-ups you cannot predict.

2. **Open with context, not with questions.** Tell the person why you are here: "We are trying to understand what mornings are actually like at the clinic. Not what the system should do — what your day feels like." This sets the frame: you are here to learn, not to validate.

3. **Anchor in a specific day.** "Can you walk me through last Tuesday morning? From the moment you arrived at the clinic." Not "a typical morning." Not "your workflow." A specific day forces memory, and memory produces detail.

4. **Follow their sequence, not your agenda.** When Ronit says "I checked the notebook," do not jump to "what about the digital system?" Stay with the notebook. Ask: "What was in it? What were you looking for? Did you find it?" Follow the person's path before redirecting to yours.

5. **Use the three question types at transitions.**
   - When they describe a step: context question. "What happens right before that?"
   - When they describe a frustration: feeling question. "What's the feeling in that moment?"
   - When they describe a workaround: surprise question. "What did you expect to happen? What actually happened?"

6. **When they offer a solution, redirect to the problem.** "I need a dashboard that shows priorities" becomes "Tell me about a morning when you didn't know the priorities. What happened?" The solution tells you what they think they want. The story tells you what they actually need.

7. **Use silence deliberately.** When the person finishes a sentence and you feel the urge to respond — wait. Count to three. Often they will add something. That addition — the thing they almost didn't say — is frequently the most important thing in the interview.

8. **Watch for the insight moment.** It sounds like a pause, then a sentence that starts with "actually..." or "I never thought about it, but..." or "huh." When the interviewee discovers something about their own experience that they hadn't articulated before, you have reached insight. Note it. Do not rush past it.

9. **Close with their language.** In the last five minutes, reflect back what you heard using their words, not yours. "So the morning is really about knowing what you're walking into — before the first patient arrives, you need to feel prepared." Let them correct you. Their corrections are data.

10. **Debrief within 30 minutes.** Sit down — alone or with one teammate — and write three things: the top 3 surprises, the top 3 pain moments, and any quotes you want to keep verbatim. After 30 minutes, your memory smooths the edges. You need the edges.

## Common Mistakes

**Asking about the system instead of the day.** You sit down and say "so, how do you use the scheduling feature?" Ronit gives you a polite, useless answer about how the scheduling feature works. You learn nothing about the three places she checks before the scheduling feature, or the fact that she often skips it entirely. Start with the day, not the tool.

**Filling the silence.** Ronit pauses. You panic. You say "so you mean something like...?" and offer a hypothesis. She agrees because it is easier than thinking. You leave believing you understood. You understood your own projection. The silence was where the truth was forming.

**Taking notes on a laptop.** The screen becomes a wall between you and the person. They watch you type and start editing themselves — shorter answers, less vulnerability, more polished descriptions of what they do. Use a paper notepad. Write fragments, not sentences. The transcript is not the point. The fragments trigger memory; the memory is the point.

**Running through your question list.** You prepared seven questions and you are going to ask all seven, in order. By question four, the interview feels like a checklist. The interviewee is performing answers. The best interviews use two or three prepared questions and fifteen follow-ups that no one could have predicted.

**Interviewing in a group.** Three people from your team sit across from one domain expert. The expert performs confidence because they have an audience. The interviewers compete for airtime. One interviewer, one note-taker. That is the maximum. If you need more people to hear the insights, record the session (with consent) and play it back.

## From the Novel

::: details Chapter 5 — Alignment
Lior interviews Avi about Ronit's Tuesday morning. The conversation starts with Michal's careful orchestration — a thirty-minute "dashboard alignment" call. It could have been a feature negotiation. Instead, Lior asks:

> "When Ronit opens the app on Tuesday morning — what does success look like? Not for the app. For her. What's the feeling at eight fifty-eight, before the first patient comes in, if everything has gone right?"

This question works because of what it refuses to do. It does not ask about features. It does not ask about the system. It asks about a person, at a specific time, in a specific emotional state. It redirects Avi from describing what the dashboard should show to describing what Ronit should feel. That redirection — from system to person, from feature to feeling — is the fundamental move of a discovery interview.

Avi's answer is the insight: "She feels like she knows what she's walking into. That's all. She just — knows. Without having to look in three places." Lior writes three words: *Knows. One place. Without effort.* That is the design specification. Not a feature list. Not a wireframe. A feeling, made specific enough to build toward.

Notice what Lior does before asking that question. He has already studied the wireframes. He has already looked at what he built. He comes prepared — but he does not lead with his preparation. He leads with curiosity. And notice what happens when Avi asks "how long to change that?" — the moment where a less disciplined interviewer would give a number. Lior says "I don't know yet. Because I think the prioritization logic depends on how you define urgent." He resists the pull of progress in order to stay in the problem.

That resistance — the refusal to move to solution before the problem is fully understood — is the hardest skill in discovery interviewing. The room wants a number. The PM wants a timeline. The founder wants to know it is going to be okay. And the interviewer's job is to say: not yet.
:::

::: details Chapter 14 — The Language of the Domain
The domain workshop brings the real Ronit into the room. Eran facilitates, and the key moment comes during timeline sequencing. Eran asks Ronit when she reviews patient history. She says "morning, before the first patient." He asks "always?" She pauses. "When I can. When the system shows me I need to."

Then Eran's pivotal question: "And when does the system show you?"

Another pause. Longer. "It doesn't. I check."

Lior writes: *Vet checks. System doesn't prompt. History retrieval is manual, triggered by vet memory, not by system awareness.*

This is a textbook example of the surprise question — "what did you expect vs. what actually happened?" — applied not to the user but to the system's role. Eran does not ask "what should the system do?" He follows the workflow until the gap reveals itself. The system's job, he then articulates, "is not to give you history when you ask for it. It's to make sure you've seen what you need before you need it." That insight — anticipatory vs. reactive — came from following the timeline, not from asking about features.

The technique: do not ask what the system should do. Map what the person does, step by step, and notice where the system is absent. The absence is the insight.

Later, Sigal the receptionist describes the emergency walk-in problem — "show me where there's flexibility" — and Avi says quietly: "I designed the system from the vet's perspective. The receptionist's problem — I didn't spend enough time with Sigal." This is the second payoff of good interviewing: you discover users you did not know existed. You cannot plan for this. You can only create the conditions where it can happen — by following the workflow instead of the feature list.
:::

## When This Breaks Down

**The interviewee is not the actual user.** You are interviewing a manager who describes what their team does, not a person who does the work. The manager's description is a model — simplified, idealized, missing the workarounds. If you cannot interview the person who does the work, name what you are getting: secondhand data. It is useful, but it is not discovery. Push to get in the room with Ronit, not just Avi.

**The interviewee is performing for their boss.** Avi is in the room. Ronit is polite. She describes the workarounds in general terms, avoids criticizing the system Avi designed, and gives shorter answers than she would give privately. If the power dynamic cannot be removed, do two interviews: one with the stakeholder present (for alignment), one without (for truth). The truth interview is the one you build from.

**You are interviewing about a future state, not a current one.** "Walk me through last Tuesday" only works if Tuesday has already happened. If you are designing for a new domain — one where nobody has a current workflow — you cannot anchor in a specific day. Instead, anchor in the closest analog: "Tell me about the last time you had to make a decision with incomplete information." The technique still works; the anchor is different.
