# The Designer in Discovery

<span class="part-label">Part 3 — Design Craft</span>

::: tip When this chapter is for
There is nothing to design yet. The team is talking about problems, running discovery, mapping journeys — and the designer is sitting there wondering when the real work starts.
:::

## The Direct Answer

- **Your first week on a new project: observe. Do not deliver.** Sit in refinement, Three Amigos, standups. Listen for the shape of the problem before you propose a shape for the solution.
- **Sketch to think, not to present.** Grab a marker and draw the current state — what happens today, without your product. This is a thinking tool, not a deliverable. It dies on the whiteboard and that is its job.
- **Be present at three moments, every time:** problem definition (before wireframes exist), implementation (checking that intent matches build), and testing (understanding whether the design caused the bug Shira found).
- **Enter teams that already have momentum by joining, not imposing.** Ask questions about what they've learned. Contribute observations. Earn the right to reshape by first understanding the shape that exists.
- **Stop measuring yourself by finished artifacts.** The portfolio trap is real: twelve beautiful case studies, each one a finished answer to a question somebody else framed. Your value starts at the question.

## Why This Works

Design is pattern recognition applied to human experience. You cannot recognize patterns you have not observed. When Yoav sat in his first refinement session, he watched Shira find an edge case in seven minutes that had taken Lior a full day to discover in the field. He watched Oren sketch something on the whiteboard that was not a wireframe and not an architecture diagram but something in between — a thinking tool, disposable and functional. Yoav learned more about the product's actual shape in that session than he would have from a week of reading documentation.

The observation week is not busy-work. It is the fastest way to build the internal model that makes design decisions good. Without it, you are designing from the spec, and the spec is always an abstraction of the problem. You need the problem itself — the texture of how Tamar runs refinement, which edge cases Shira finds first, where Oren reaches for the whiteboard because words are failing. These are design inputs. You just have to be in the room to receive them.

Most design processes are linear: brief, research, wireframe, high-fidelity, handoff. Each stage completes before the next begins. The designer's contribution arrives as a finished artifact rather than a developing conversation. This feels clean. It is also the reason teams build beautiful things that miss the point. The wireframe encodes assumptions about the solution. Development builds to the wireframe. The assumptions never get questioned because they are embedded in something that looks finished.

"Design throughout, not design as a phase" means practically this: you are present when the problem is defined (you sketch the current state, you map the journey, you hold the experience as a whole). You are present during implementation (you check whether what Lior built matches what was intended — not pixel-perfect, but intent-matching). You are present during testing (when Shira finds a bug, you ask: did the design cause this? Is the flow itself wrong, or is this an implementation miss?). Three touchpoints. Every story. Not as a bottleneck — as a perspective.

## Step by Step

### First week on a new project

1. **Do not open Figma.** Do not sketch solutions. Do not audit the existing UI. Not yet.
2. **Attend every ceremony the team runs.** Standup, refinement, Three Amigos, retro. Sit. Listen. Take notes about what confuses you.
3. **Map the current state.** By Thursday, draw the flow as it exists today — the user's experience without your improvements. Share it. Ask: "Did I get this right?" The corrections are the real output.
4. **Identify the three most painful moments in the current experience.** Not from the backlog. From what you heard in the room.
5. **By Friday, you should be able to answer:** Who is the user (by name and context)? What does their Tuesday actually look like? Where does the current product help, and where does it fail?

### Ongoing: the three design checkpoints

6. **At problem definition** (before any wireframe exists): participate in discovery or refinement. Sketch the problem space. Map what exists. Ask: "What does Dina do right now, without this feature?"
7. **At implementation** (during the sprint): check in with the developer building the story. Look at what is emerging. Ask: "Does this match what we intended?" Not pixel review — intent review.
8. **At testing** (when QA runs scenarios): look at what Shira finds. Ask: "Is this a code bug or a design bug? Did we design a flow that a real person would not follow?"

### Sketch vs. wireframe — knowing which moment you are in

9. **Sketch when you are thinking.** Pen, whiteboard, back of a napkin. The purpose is to externalize a question. It should take under five minutes and it should be throwaway. If you are tempted to straighten the lines, you have crossed into wireframing.
10. **Wireframe when you have a validated problem and a chosen approach.** The Feature Brief exists. The Core Trio has agreed on direction. Now you are communicating a solution, not exploring a question.
11. **Use the design system when implementing known patterns.** The button exists. The form layout exists. Do not redesign them. Reach for the system.
12. **Sketch fresh when the pattern does not exist.** If the design system has no component for this interaction, that is a signal. Explore before you systematize.

## Common Mistakes

**Arriving with solutions.** Yoav's instinct was to look at the existing UI, identify problems, and propose improvements. That is studio thinking — the client gives you a brief, you give them an answer. In a product team, the brief is still forming. If you arrive with answers, you close the space the team needs to find the right question.

**Waiting to be invited.** The opposite failure. The team has momentum. Nobody has mentioned design. You sit politely and wait for someone to say "we need a wireframe." By the time they say it, the assumptions are already baked. Enter by contributing observations, not by waiting for a request.

**Treating the observation week as downtime.** Tamar insisted on it. Yoav agreed with the grace of someone who didn't fully believe in it. By Thursday he understood more about the product than his portfolio review had told him. The observation week is the highest-leverage time a designer spends on a project, and it feels like doing nothing. That feeling is a trap.

**Measuring yourself by artifacts.** Twelve projects in the portfolio. Each one photographed with care. Clean interfaces. Considered typography. Each one a finished answer to a question that had been asked without the designer in the room. The portfolio trap is not about vanity — it is about how you define your own contribution. If your value is "I make things look good," you will always arrive after the important decisions. If your value is "I hold the experience as a whole," you arrive at the beginning.

**Confusing intent review with pixel review.** When you check implementation, you are not looking for whether the border radius matches the Figma file. You are looking for whether the flow makes sense, whether the information hierarchy works, whether a real person would navigate this the way you intended. Pixel review happens too. But intent review is the one that catches direction errors.

## From the Novel

::: details Chapter 18 — Yoav
Yoav arrives with a laptop, a drawing tablet, and the specific confidence of someone who has been told he is good at his job often enough to stop questioning it. His portfolio is real — twelve projects, clean interfaces, considered typography. He had worked at a studio with a process that moved in one direction: brief to research to wireframes to mockups to handoff. He was good at that flow. He is about to discover that the flow is the problem.

**The observation week.** Tamar insists Yoav spend his first week observing. He agrees without fully believing in it. He sits in refinement. Watches Tamar walk through a story — "not a finished story, a problem statement, rough, with questions still visible in it." Watches Shira find an edge case in seven minutes. Watches Oren sketch something on the whiteboard that is "not a wireframe and not an architecture diagram but something between them, a thinking-tool, quick and functional and disposable."

The craft: Tamar knows that a designer who starts by designing will design from assumptions. She buys him a week of observation so his first real contribution comes from understanding, not instinct. This is an investment most teams skip — and it is why most designers spend their first month reworking things they got wrong in week one.

**"When does design come in?"** On Thursday, Yoav asks Tamar the question. She turns it back: "What do you think?" He gives the studio answer — at the wireframe stage. She asks what is wrong with that. He thinks about Avi's forty-seven Figma screens, built before anyone watched Dina work. "The problem is that by the wireframe stage someone has already made assumptions about the solution. And the wireframe encodes those assumptions." Tamar's answer: "Before the wireframe. And also later."

The craft: Tamar does not lecture. She asks Yoav to discover the answer himself, because a discovered answer changes behavior and a delivered answer gets forgotten. When she adds "and also later," she names the two moments most teams miss: design at problem definition and design at testing. The wireframe moment is the one everybody remembers. The other two are where design actually prevents waste.

**The portfolio realization.** Yoav thinks about his twelve projects. "Each one a finished answer to a question that had been asked without him in the room." This is the moment his professional identity shifts — from someone who makes beautiful artifacts to someone who shapes how problems are understood.

The craft: If you recognize yourself in Yoav's portfolio, the fix is not to stop making beautiful work. It is to start earlier. Ask to be in the room when the problem is defined. Ask to observe the user before the brief is written. The artifacts will still be beautiful. They will also be right.
:::

## When This Breaks Down

**The team does not do discovery.** If stories arrive as tickets with no problem context, the designer has nothing to observe and nowhere to contribute early. The fix is not to demand a new process — it is to start asking one question per story: "What does the user do today without this?" Even in a ticket-driven team, that question opens a crack for design thinking.

**The pace does not allow observation.** Some teams ship daily. There is no week to sit and watch. In that case, compress: attend one refinement, one standup, one testing session. Map the current state in two hours, not five days. The principle holds — understand before you propose — but the timeline shrinks.

**The designer is the only one who cares about experience.** If Shira is not asking "would Dina actually do this?" and Lior is not asking "does this match what we intended?" — the designer becomes the sole voice for the user, which is unsustainable. This is a team problem, not a design problem. Raise it in retro. The goal is not "designers own the user" but "everyone holds the experience."
