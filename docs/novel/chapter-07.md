# Chapter Seven — Velocity

<span class="part-label">Part One — Upstream</span>

The sprint board had a satisfying quality that Michal had learned, over years, to distrust.

Green tickets. Moving tickets. The daily increment of cards sliding from In Progress to Done with the smooth inevitability of a well-oiled thing. Velocity, the metric said. Twenty-two points last sprint. Twenty-six the sprint before. The numbers in a column, rising.

She looked at the board every morning the way you check the weather — not because you control it, but because it tells you something about what to carry.

This morning it told her: three stories done, two in progress, one blocked.

What it didn't tell her — what no board had ever been designed to tell her — was whether the done things were the right things.

She had been thinking about this since Thursday. Since the call with Lior and Avi. Since the moment Lior had asked what does success feel like for Ronit and Avi had answered and she had watched something shift between them — a small tectonic thing, two plates finding a new position — and had felt, sitting in the middle of it, a complicated emotion she was still disaggregating.

Part of it was relief. She recognized this, examined it, put it aside.

Part of it was something that felt uncomfortably like: *this should have happened in week one.*

And underneath that, the question she hadn't asked aloud: *whose job was it to make it happen?*

She opened the sprint board again.

Three stories done.

> As a user I can view my appointments.

Done.

> As a user I can filter appointments by date.

Done.

> As a user I can see patient name and appointment type.

Done.

She read them the way Lior had taught her, without meaning to, to read them — not as checkboxes but as experiences. As Ronit, on a Tuesday morning, seven minutes before the clinic opened.

I can view my appointments. Yes. In a list. Chronological. All fourteen of them, equal weight, the emergency fourth from the top.

I can filter by date. Yes, with a date picker in the top right corner that required three taps and a scroll.

I can see patient name and appointment type. Yes. In eight-point font.

She thought about Ronit. The seven minutes. *She just knows.*

She closed the board.

This was the problem with velocity as a metric — it measured the speed of the vehicle without asking whether the vehicle was on the right road. They were moving. Twenty-two points, twenty-six points, the numbers rising with the clean momentum of a thing that has found its rhythm. And the rhythm was real — the team was working, genuinely working, Lior and Oren and Michal herself, all of them moving — but movement in a system without a destination is just a very organized way of going nowhere.

She opened a new document.

Typed, then deleted:

> We need to talk about direction.

Typed, then kept:

> Retrospective agenda — Friday.

She had been running retrospectives for eleven weeks. They followed a format she had learned at a conference in 2019 — a facilitator had used sticky notes and a timer and the energy of someone who genuinely believed that structured reflection produced change. What went well. What didn't. What we'll do differently.

Eleven retrospectives. The what we'll do differently column, each week, a slightly different version of the same things. Better communication. Clearer tickets. Earlier flagging of blockers.

And then the next week, the same board, the same motion, the slightly wrong thing built slightly faster.

She stared at the document.

Thought: *a retrospective about the process of building the wrong thing is still a retrospective about the wrong thing.*

Her phone buzzed.
