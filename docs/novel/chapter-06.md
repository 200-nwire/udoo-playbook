# Chapter Six — Eran Writes a Document

It started small.

A Confluence page, blank, cursor blinking. The title he had typed and deleted three times before settling on something plain: Strato — Production Environment: What You Need to Know.

He had been sitting with it for twenty minutes, which was longer than it had taken him to find and fix the original bug. This struck him as information about something.

The problem was not knowing what to write. He knew exactly what to write. The problem was something underneath the writing — a resistance he was trying to locate before he could dissolve it. Something about the act of writing it all down that felt, in a way he was still circling, like loss.

He thought about what Yossi had said in the corridor. He needs the slide to be clean.

He thought about the Tuesday in April, the payment gateway, the 8000.

He thought: I have been, without meaning to, the person who keeps the lights on. And I have liked being that person. And writing this document is the beginning of not being that person anymore.

He started writing.

PAYMENT_TIMEOUT_MS — Production value: 8000. Staging value: 3000. Why they differ:

And then the reason, fully, in plain language, with the name of the support engineer at the payment gateway who had explained it, and the date, and the behavior you would observe if you got it wrong, and what to do if you observed that behavior.

Then the next thing. The deployment sequence. Step by step, in order, with the specific commands, the specific environment, the specific things that would go wrong and had gone wrong and how to recover from them.

Then the next thing.

He wrote for an hour. Then two.

Around noon Yossi appeared at his desk with two coffees, looked at the screen, looked at Eran, said nothing, put a coffee down, and left.

Eran kept writing.

By three in the afternoon he had twelve pages. Not twelve pages of padding — twelve pages of things that lived, until today, only in his head. The quirks and histories and invisible decisions that had accumulated over eight months of working close to a system, the sediment of daily problem-solving that had never been given a surface to settle on.

He read it back.

It was strange, reading it. Like meeting yourself in a mirror that shows you a different angle — familiar but rearranged. Here was his knowledge, extracted and organized and laid flat, and it was both more and less than he'd thought. More, because there was so much of it, because even as he wrote he kept remembering things he'd forgotten he knew. Less, because written down it looked like information rather than mastery. Like something anyone could learn. Like something someone could pick up and use on a Tuesday morning when he was somewhere else.

He had thought the writing would feel like loss. It felt, instead, like something he didn't have a word for yet. Not relief exactly. More like — setting something down. Something he hadn't known he'd been carrying because he'd been carrying it so long.

He tagged Yossi in the document.

Then, after a moment, he tagged the whole team.

Then he sat back and drank the cold coffee and watched the dust in the afternoon light and thought about Lior, somewhere in another city, building something, and wondered whether Lior knew yet what Eran had just started to understand.

That the knowledge was never supposed to be yours.

That keeping it had felt like strength and was actually, quietly, a kind of fear.

That the document was not the thing you wrote after you understood the system.

It was the beginning of sharing it — which was different from understanding it alone, in ways he was only beginning to see.
