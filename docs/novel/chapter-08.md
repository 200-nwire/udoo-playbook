# Chapter Eight — The Offer

Avi's office was smaller than Lior had imagined from the video calls — a converted apartment, as he'd thought, but intimate in a way the screen hadn't conveyed. Books on every surface, not decoratively but functionally, with the creased spines and Post-it flags of books that had been used. A whiteboard covered in writing that had been partially erased and written over, the ghost of previous thoughts visible underneath the current ones like a palimpsest.

Lior was there in person for the first time. Michal beside him. Avi across the table, with the quality of attention he'd shown on the Thursday call — present in the specific way of someone who has learned, expensively, that half-attention is its own kind of waste.

"I'll get to it," Avi said. "I had a meeting yesterday. One of the clinic chains — Vetcare. You know them?"

Michal nodded. Lior didn't, but he filed it.

"Forty-two clinics. They're the second largest chain in the country. Their current system is —" Avi made a gesture that encompassed the entire history of enterprise veterinary software. "They've been looking to move for two years. They've talked to four vendors. None of them could show them what they actually needed." He paused. "I showed them the demo. The Thursday recording you sent."

Lior kept his face still.

The Thursday recording. The two minutes forty seconds. The chronological list. The neutral dashboard. The thing he had watched three days ago with the quiet dismay of a person seeing clearly, for the first time, what he'd built.

"They want a pilot," Avi said. "Six clinics. Three months. If it works, they roll out across all forty-two." He let that land. "This is the contract that funds the next three years, Michal. This is the thing."

Silence.

Outside the window, a city went about its business, magnificently unaware that in this room a number was hovering that had the power to change the shape of the next several years of several people's lives.

"That's extraordinary," Michal said, in the voice she used when she was thinking fast and needed time. "Congratulations, Avi. That's — when do they need an answer?"

"Three weeks."

"And the pilot — what would they need to see?"

"The dashboard." Avi looked at Lior. "The real one. The one we talked about on Thursday. The one that shows Ronit what Tuesday is."

Lior thought about the notepad. The questions. One place. Without effort. She just knows.

He thought about what he'd built. The chronological list. The eight-point font.

He thought about the gap between those two things — not a technical gap, not primarily, but a gap of understanding, of knowing what the problem actually was, of having been given eleven weeks to build a thing and having spent them building a version of it that was directionally correct and substantively wrong.

Three weeks.

"Lior." Avi's voice was careful. "Tell me honestly. What do we have, and what would we need?"

The room was very quiet. Michal beside him, still, in the stillness of someone managing the gap between what they want to hear and what they're about to hear.

Lior thought of all the ways to answer the question.

He thought of the sprint board. The twenty-six points of velocity. The green tickets. As a user I can view my appointments.

He thought of Eran, somewhere, writing a document that nobody had asked him to write.

He thought of Ronit. Seven minutes. Tuesday morning.

"We have motion," he said. "We don't have the right thing yet."

Avi nodded once, slowly, like a man confirming something he already knew.

"How long for the right thing?"

"I don't know. Because I don't know enough yet. I know more than I knew two weeks ago and not enough for what you're describing."

Michal made a sound that was not quite a word.

"What do you need to know?" Avi said.

"I need to meet Ronit."

A beat.

"The actual Ronit," Lior said. "A vet. In a clinic. On a Tuesday morning. I need to watch her work for two hours and not ask her what she wants — just watch what she does. Because what people say they want and what would actually help them —" He stopped. Tried again. "The wireframes you made were good. They were really good. But they were made from the outside. I need to see it from the inside."

Avi looked at him for a long moment.

"I can arrange that," he said quietly. "I know a clinic in Ramat Gan. The head vet has been using the beta. She has opinions."

"Good," Lior said. "I want the opinions."

Michal was writing something. Lior couldn't see what. Her pen moved with the focused energy of someone converting a conversation into a plan, which was her gift — the ability to take the shapeless and give it a schedule, which was valuable and also, he was beginning to understand, not sufficient on its own.

"Three weeks," she said. "If Lior goes to the clinic Monday. We run an emergency sprint. We demo Thursday week two. Buffer week three." She looked up. "It's possible."

"With what we know now, it isn't," Lior said. Not unkindly. Just plainly.

She looked at him.

"After Monday, maybe. Depending on what I find. But I need Monday first before I can tell you what's possible. Planning around what we don't know yet is how we got here."

The sentence arrived in the room and stayed there.

Avi picked up his coffee. Put it down without drinking.

"He's right," he said to Michal. Gently. "He's right."

Michal looked at the plan she'd been writing. The schedule, the sprints, the buffer week. The clean forward motion of a project that knew where it was going.

She put the pen down.

"Monday," she said. "Then we plan."

It cost her something. Lior could see that. Not stubbornness — something more like the professional vertigo of a person who has organized their competence around a particular set of tools and is being asked, politely but clearly, to put them down and look at the situation first.

He understood it. He had felt it himself, every morning for eleven weeks, opening Jira and finding his competence in building things that had been requested rather than in questioning whether they should be built.

They were the same, he thought. All of them — Michal, himself, probably Eran, probably Dani with his spreadsheet. All of them competent in the same direction. All of them moving.

None of them, until very recently, asking where.
