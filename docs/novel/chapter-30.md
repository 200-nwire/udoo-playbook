# Chapter 30 — The Clinic That Left

The message came from Netta on a Thursday.

Not in the team channel. In a direct message to Tamar and Lior, which meant it was something she had decided needed to reach them before it reached everyone.

Refu'at HaNefesh dropped out of the pilot. They've moved to a competitor. I'm going to call them tomorrow to understand why. Wanted you to know before the call.

Lior read it at his desk. Felt the specific quality of this information — not panic, something slower and more instructive. A clinic had left. A real clinic, with real vets, who had tried the product and chosen to leave.

He typed back: I want to be on that call if they'll allow it.

Netta: I'll ask. No promises — they might not want to talk.

They talked.

The call was twenty minutes. The clinic manager, a man named Roi, who had the quality of someone who had made a decision he was comfortable with and was willing to explain it without rancor.

The morning prep was good, he said. Our vets liked it.

But, Netta said.

But our receptionist, he said. Sigal's problem — the emergency triage, the schedule flexibility. We're a high-volume clinic. Twelve vets. On a busy Tuesday we have four or five emergency walk-ins before noon. The system doesn't handle that. Our receptionist was managing it on a whiteboard beside the screen because the screen couldn't tell her what she needed to know.

We know that problem, Lior said. He said it carefully, aware of the irony. It's in our discovery pipeline.

I know, Roi said. The competitor has a version of it. Rough — not as good as yours will probably be. But it exists now, and the whiteboard was costing us patient trust and receptionist sanity.

A silence.

When did you decide? Netta said.

Six weeks ago.

That's week two of the pilot.

Yes.

Why didn't you raise it with us?

A pause.

Honestly? Roi said. We did. In the onboarding session. We mentioned it. It was acknowledged and we were told it was on the roadmap. Another pause. On the roadmap is where things go to wait.

Lior closed his eyes for a moment.

Netta was writing. He could hear the pen.

What would have needed to be different? she said.

If someone had come and watched us work, Roi said. Simply, without accusation. The way you did, apparently, with HaRefu'ah. If someone had watched Sigal for a morning — not in week eight, in week one. She could have told you in an hour what it took us six weeks to demonstrate by leaving.

The call ended.

Netta sat with the recording for a long time.

Then she wrote the feedback log entry. The most detailed one she had written.

Clinic: Refu'at HaNefesh. Status: churned. Reason: emergency triage gap — Sigal's problem unaddressed. Duration of pain: pilot start. Signal raised: week two, onboarding session. Disposition of signal: acknowledged, added to roadmap. Outcome: signal did not enter product planning. Clinic left in week eight.

She added:

Revenue lost: one clinic. Revenue at risk: every high-volume clinic in the Vetcare rollout.

Then:

Root cause: the signal had a path into the conversation but not into the system. "On the roadmap" is not a path. It is the place where signals go when no one has decided what to do with them.

She tagged Tamar.

Added one more line:

Scheduling intelligence is no longer a future initiative. It is a present risk. Recommend: move discovery sprint to this month, before full rollout.

She sat back.

Thought about Roi's words. If someone had come and watched us work.

Thought about Lior, in Ramat Gan, on a Monday morning, watching Dina work, filling a notebook with moments rather than features.

The knowledge had existed. Sigal's problem had been visible from day one. The signal had been raised in week two. The path from that signal to a developer's understanding had not existed, and so the knowledge had sat in an onboarding session summary, in a roadmap column, visible and inert.

She thought: a feedback loop that stops at acknowledgment is not a loop. It's a line. It goes in and doesn't come back.

She opened a new document.

Not a feedback log entry. Something else.

At the top she wrote: How a signal becomes a sprint.

Below it she drew the path. Every step. Who owns each step. How long each step should take. What blocks it. What breaks it.

She had not been asked to write this. Nobody had given her the mandate. But she had spent six years in customer success watching signals disappear into systems that didn't know what to do with them, and she had found, in the last six weeks, a team that was trying to build the path. She was going to write down what the path looked like before the next signal got lost.

She worked for two hours.

When she was done she had a document that was half process and half philosophy — the process for how signals moved, and the reasoning for why each step was necessary, and what happened when it was skipped.

She sent it to Tamar.

Tamar read it in twenty minutes.

Sent back two words: This is upstream.

Netta looked at the message.

Typed: What do you mean?

Tamar: I mean this document is the thing that connects Offstream to Upstream. You've just written the missing page. Can we talk tomorrow?
