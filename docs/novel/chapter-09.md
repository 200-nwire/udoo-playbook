# Chapter Nine — The Sprint That Solved Nothing

<span class="part-label">Part One — Upstream</span>

They ran the sprint anyway.

This was Michal's decision, and it was not a wrong decision — there were real things to build, real progress to make, and the alternative to the sprint was a kind of organizational paralysis that would help no one. So she ran it, with the precision she brought to everything: planning session Monday morning, daily standups, the board with its columns and its WIP limits and its tickets flowing from left to right with the satisfying motion of water finding level.

Lior was in Ramat Gan on Monday.

The clinic was called HaRefu'ah. A single-story building with a mural of animals on the outside wall — a dog, a cat, something that might have been a rabbit — painted by someone with more enthusiasm than technique. Inside, the smell of antiseptic and something warmer underneath it. Animals in small cages in a room off the corridor, the intermittent sounds of them, the presence of lives being tended.

The vet's name was Dina.

She was forty-three, had been a vet for sixteen years, had agreed to let Lior shadow her with the slightly baffled generosity of someone who has been asked an unusual favor and said yes because she couldn't think of a reason not to. She handed him a coffee without asking if he wanted one and told him to stay out of the way and ask questions at the end.

He stayed out of the way.

He watched.

He had brought a notebook. By nine-fifteen it was full.

Not of features. Not of requirements. Of moments — the specific texture of Dina's Tuesday morning, the way she moved through the space, what she reached for first, what she ignored, where she slowed, where her face changed.

Eight forty-seven: she arrived. Did not open the app. Opened a physical folder on the reception desk — handwritten notes from the overnight answering service — and read it standing up, coat still on.

Eight fifty-two: she opened the app. Scrolled the appointment list. Stopped at the fourth entry. Frowned. Tapped through to the patient record. Tapped back.

"What happened there?" Lior asked, before he remembered he wasn't supposed to ask yet.

She glanced at him. "Mitzpeh. He's a cat. Came in last month with a UTI. He's back today and I can't remember —" She tapped again. "I need to see the last visit notes before he comes in. The owner gets anxious if I ask the same questions twice."

She found the notes. Read them. The frown eased.

Nine-oh-three: first patient. A golden retriever named Boaz with the specific energy of a dog that does not understand that the vet's office is a place where dignity is expected. His owner — a man in his sixties, gentle, worried — sat in the plastic chair and held the lead with both hands.

Lior watched Dina work. The simultaneous attention of it — listening to the owner, examining the dog, her hand on the dog's flank, her eyes moving between the animal and the screen, entering notes in a way that looked like thinking rather than typing.

At nine forty-seven, between patients, she turned to him.

"You're not writing features," she said. It wasn't a question.

"No," he said.

"What are you writing?"

He looked at the notebook. Arrives before app. Physical folder first. Last visit notes before anxious owners. The frown.

"I'm writing Tuesday," he said.

She looked at him for a moment with the assessing quality of someone who has spent sixteen years reading animals and has become very good at reading other things too.

"The system your company is building," she said. "Does it know that I need last visit notes before I go into the room?"

"Not yet."

"Does it know that Mitzpeh's owner calls if he doesn't get a follow-up message by six pm?"

"No."

"Does it know that I have three vets on Tuesdays and four on Thursdays and the appointment capacity is completely different and the way I organize my morning is completely different?"

"No."

She nodded slowly. Not with frustration — with the recognition of a professional who has been given inadequate tools for long enough that she has stopped expecting adequate ones.

"What does it know?" she said.

He thought about the sprint board. The green tickets. As a user I can view my appointments.

"It knows your appointments," he said. "In order."

She almost smiled. "That's a start," she said. "Thirteen years ago I had a paper list. This is better than a paper list."

"It should be better than better than a paper list."

She looked at him again with that assessing quality.

"Yes," she said. "It should."

---

Back in the office — his kitchen, his laptop, the Eilat photo — Lior opened Jira.

The sprint was running. Three stories in progress. Oren had moved two tickets to done. The velocity was on track for another twenty-something points.

He looked at the tickets.

> As a user I can sort appointments by status.

> As a user I can add a note to an appointment.

> As a user I can view patient history.

He thought about Dina. The physical folder. The frown at the fourth entry. *I need to see the last visit notes before he comes in.*

As a user I can view patient history was in the done column. He clicked it. The implementation showed a separate screen, accessible from the patient profile, three taps from the appointment list.

Three taps. When Dina had seven minutes and a coat still on.

He sat with this for a long time.

Then he opened a new document. Not Jira. Not Confluence. Just a blank page.

He wrote at the top:

> What Dina's Tuesday actually needs.

And underneath it, for the first time in eleven weeks, he wrote not a list of features but a description of a morning. A real morning, with a real person, with the specific texture of what it felt like to do a hard job well when the tools cooperated and badly when they didn't.

He wrote for an hour.

When he stopped he read it back and felt something he hadn't felt in a long time about this project.

He felt like he knew what he was building.

Not the whole thing. Not the Vetcare pilot, not the forty-two clinics, not the three-year contract. Just the next thing. The small, specific, right next thing.

He opened Jira.

Looked at the sprint.

Looked at the three stories in progress — the sorting, the notes, the history screen that was three taps from where Dina needed it.

Opened a message to Michal.

Typed:

> I need to talk to you before we go any further with the sprint. I know this is bad timing. But I think we're building the wrong things again, and I'd rather say it now.

He looked at the message.

Added:

> I also think I know what the right things are. Finally.

Sent it.

Closed Jira.

Opened the document about Dina's Tuesday.

And started, for the first time, to write something that felt like the beginning of the actual work.
