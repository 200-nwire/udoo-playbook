# Chapter Four — The Client

<span class="part-label">Part One — Upstream</span>

Avi Shapiro had built three companies.

The first had failed in the way first companies fail — gradually, then all at once, with the specific dignity of a man who had learned something expensive and was determined not to show how expensive. The second had been acquired, which sounds like success and mostly was, except that the acquiring company had been larger and slower and had absorbed his product the way a large slow thing absorbs a small fast thing — completely, without malice, leaving no trace.

The third was Kelev. A platform for veterinary clinics. Appointment management, medical records, billing, client communication — the infrastructure of care for the people who care for animals, which was a sentence Avi had used in three pitch decks and still believed, on good days.

He was fifty-one. He had the hands of someone who had spent years building things — not literally, not with wood and nails, but the metaphorical equivalent, the hands of a person who has learned that things require maintenance and that maintenance requires attention and that attention is the thing most people are least willing to give.

He opened his laptop in the corner of the coffee shop he used as a second office. The real office was twenty minutes away, a converted apartment with four employees who were, this Tuesday morning, doing things he trusted them to do because the alternative — watching them do things — had stopped working for him around company two.

He opened the Figma file.

He had made these wireframes himself. Six weeks ago, on a Sunday evening, with the focused pleasure of a man who knows what he wants and has found a way to show it. Every screen annotated. Every flow connected. The dashboard in the center — the thing Lior was building, the thing Michal reported on every week with the words progressing well — showing, in clean simple frames, exactly what a vet should see when they opened the app on a busy Tuesday morning.

He looked at it for a long time.

Then he opened the last demo video Michal had sent. Recorded by someone — Lior, he assumed, though he had never spoken to Lior directly — three weeks ago. Two minutes and forty seconds of a screen recording with no audio, showing a component that looked like a dashboard, technically, the way a sketch looks like a portrait — the correct number of features in roughly the correct positions, the proportions slightly wrong in ways he couldn't immediately name.

He watched it twice.

Then he opened a new message to Michal.

Typed:

> Can I talk directly to the developer?

Looked at the message for a moment.

Deleted directly — it implied something he didn't mean to imply.

Sent:

> Can I talk to the developer? I have some thoughts on the dashboard I want to share.

He closed the laptop and drank his coffee and watched the street outside, where a woman was standing at the corner looking at her phone with the expression of someone who has just received news that requires a moment to absorb before the next thing can happen.

He thought about the word progressing.

He had been told the project was progressing for eleven weeks. He had received weekly updates with percentages that had climbed, steadily, from twenty to forty to sixty to eighty-three, and had felt, watching those numbers rise, a growing unease he hadn't been able to name until now, watching the two-minute forty-second video of a dashboard that was eighty-three percent of the wrong thing.

The unease was this: progress was a description of motion, not direction.

He had been watching motion. He had never confirmed direction.

His phone buzzed. Michal: Of course! I'll set something up.

He looked at the message. Felt the specific texture of being managed by someone who was very good at their job and whose job was, in part, to manage him. Not badly. Genuinely. Michal was not an obstacle — she was a person working very hard to keep something moving that had no clear idea where it was going.

Which meant that when it arrived somewhere, it would arrive somewhere.

He just didn't know where.
