# Chapter 35 — The New Project

<span class="part-label">Part Four — Offstream</span>

Lior was in a conference room in a building he didn't know yet, in a city forty minutes from the one he'd spent the last two years in. The client was a logistics company. The product was a driver coordination system. The problem was real — he had read the brief on the train and felt, reading it, the specific quality of a problem that had been described by someone who understood it.

The team lead's name was Shai. Thirty-two, product background, the kind of person who had read the right things and had not yet had enough experience to know which of the right things was right for this specific situation.

He had a sprint board ready. Jira, organized, the tickets color-coded, the columns labelled with the reassuring vocabulary of a person who had a system.

— Week one, Shai said. We'll run a planning session Monday, establish velocity baseline, get the first sprint underway. I've pre-loaded the backlog with the client's requirements.

Lior looked at the board.

Thirty-seven tickets. Solution-named, feature-shaped, userless. *Implement route optimization. Add driver status tracking. Build dispatch interface.*

He felt, in his body, the weight of what he was looking at.

Not despair. Something more like recognition — the recognition of someone who has been in this place before and knows what it costs and also knows what it costs to say the true thing early rather than late.

He thought about Roi. *If someone had come and watched us work.*

He thought about Dina. *It knew about Mitzpeh before I did.*

He thought about the project lead on the way back from the clinic visit. *I thought I knew what the client needed.*

He thought about Eran, who had typed *keep going* and meant something more than encouragement.

— Shai, he said.

Shai looked at him.

— Before we run the planning session — can we spend a morning watching the drivers work?

A pause.

Shai looked at the board. The thirty-seven tickets. The sprint that was ready to start, that would generate velocity and movement and the clean forward motion of a team doing the things it had been organized to do.

— We have the requirements, he said.

— I know, Lior said. I want to see what's behind them.

A silence.

Outside the conference room, through the glass wall, the office was doing its Monday morning — people arriving, laptops opening, the specific energy of a week beginning with its particular quality of possibility and uncertainty.

Shai looked at the board.

Looked at Lior.

— How long? he said.

— A morning, Lior said. Two hours with one driver. Maybe three.

— And then we plan?

— And then we plan.

Shai thought about this. Lior could see him thinking — the slight resistance, the calculations, the pull of the existing structure against the unfamiliar request.

— Okay, Shai said.

Not convinced. Not enthusiastic. But willing.

It was enough.

— I'll call the client, Lior said. I'll set it up for Wednesday.

He opened his laptop. Not Jira. His email. He typed the client's name in the To field.

Then he stopped.

Looked up.

— One more thing, he said. The tickets. Can we hold off on treating them as the backlog until after Wednesday?

— They're already in the system.

— I know. I want us to look at them differently after Wednesday. Not throw them away — look at them through what we see. He paused. Some of them will be right. Some will be right problems with wrong solutions. Some will be solutions to problems we don't actually have. We won't know which until we've watched someone work.

Shai looked at the thirty-seven tickets.

At Lior.

— You've done this before, he said.

— I've done the wrong version of it, Lior said. This is the first time I'm trying to do it right from the beginning.

A beat.

— Okay, Shai said again. Different this time — something slightly more open in it, the okay of someone who is not yet convinced but is genuinely curious about what the next thing will be.

Lior sent the email.

---

Outside, the city was doing its Monday. A driver somewhere was navigating a route with a system that didn't quite understand his problem yet. In a clinic in Tel Aviv, a vet was hanging up her coat and reading three things on a tablet. In another office, a project manager was looking at a spreadsheet with a column that asked a question instead of recording an answer.

And in a conference room in a building he didn't know yet, Lior closed his email and sat with the quiet of a person who has learned something and is about to try to help someone else learn it, not by explaining but by going to watch someone work.

*Wednesday.*

*Two hours.*

*One driver.*

*Start there.*
