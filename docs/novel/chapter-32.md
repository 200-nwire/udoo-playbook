# Chapter 32 — Leading Signals

<span class="part-label">Part Four — Offstream</span>

Three months after the full rollout.

Netta's dashboard had forty-two rows. One per clinic. Seven columns: clinic name, weekly active rate, average session length, support tickets this month, morning prep adoption, afternoon note completion time, last contact date.

She looked at it every Monday morning the way Tamir looked at his monitoring dashboard — not for individual data points but for the shape. The pattern. The thing that was moving when everything else was still.

This Monday, one row was amber.

<div class="chat-log">

Clinic thirty-seven. Bat Yam. Weekly active rate: sixty-one percent, down from seventy-eight percent three weeks ago. Average session length: two point three minutes, down from four point one. Support tickets: zero.

</div>

Which would have looked healthy in isolation but in context of declining engagement looked like silence, the specific silence of a user who has stopped expecting the tool to help them.

She had learned, from Roi, from Dina's afternoon notes, from six months of watching signals, that zero support tickets was not evidence of satisfaction. It was evidence of accommodation. The user had found a workaround and stopped expecting better.

She called the clinic.

Not with a survey. Not with an NPS form. She called and asked: — How's Tuesday?

The clinic manager, a woman named Hila, answered with the slightly surprised quality of someone not accustomed to being called.

— Fine, she said. Then, after a pause: Actually — can I ask you something?

— Yes, Netta said.

— The system. The afternoon notes. Is there a faster way to navigate between records?

— Tell me what you're doing, Netta said.

Hila told her. A workflow issue — not a bug, a friction point, three extra taps in a sequence that happened forty times a day, which was a hundred and twenty extra taps, which was invisible until you named it and enormous once named.

— I'll have someone look at this today, Netta said. Is there anything else?

A pause.

— It's good overall, Hila said. The morning prep — my vets use it. That's not nothing. Another pause. I just — sometimes it feels like the system was built for a different clinic than mine.

— Tell me about your clinic, Netta said.

And Hila did, for twenty minutes, and Netta wrote, and the writing went into the feedback log, and the feedback log was read that afternoon by Tamar who flagged three items for the next refinement session, and one of them — the navigation friction — was in a story by Wednesday, shaped with Lior and Shira, in the Three Amigos session that now ran every Tuesday and Thursday as naturally as breathing.

The story shipped the following Monday.

Netta called Hila the following Wednesday.

— The navigation, she said. Did you see the update?

— Yes, Hila said. The surprise in her voice was not performed. Yesterday.

— Is it better?

A pause that had warmth in it.

— Yes, Hila said. It's better.

---

Netta wrote in the feedback log:

> Signal to resolution: eight days. Clinic health: improving. Next contact: four weeks.

She looked at the dashboard.

Clinic thirty-seven had moved from amber to green.

Not because a feature had been built. Because someone had called and asked how Tuesday was, and listened to the answer, and the answer had traveled — all the way from Hila's voice to Tamar's refinement session to Lior's implementation to Tamir's deployment — in eight days.

Eight days.

She thought about Strato. The signals that had accumulated in the Notion document that nobody read. The feature that would have been six days of development and had instead become a reason to leave.

She thought about the loop. Not as a diagram, not as a process document. As a felt thing — the specific satisfaction of a system that worked, that transformed signal into response, that made a vet in Bat Yam feel, on a Wednesday morning, that someone was paying attention.

She thought: *this is what the work is for.*

Not the dashboard. Not the metrics. Not the forty-two green rows.

Hila, on a Wednesday morning, opening the app and finding it slightly better than it was last week.

That.
