# Chapter 17 — The Board

The sprint ended on a Friday.

Lior looked at the board.

Nine stories completed. Velocity: thirty-one points. The highest sprint velocity in the project. Michal would put it in the report. The green column was full.

He felt nothing in particular about this.

He opened the board and looked at the completed stories with the careful attention he had been developing — not reading the tickets but reading what they represented. What had actually changed in the world as a result of these nine stories shipping.

The morning prep screen: done. Shipped to the pilot clinic on Wednesday. Ronit had used it on Thursday morning. Lior knew this because he had asked, and because Avi had messaged at nine-fourteen with a screenshot of Ronit's message: It knew about Mitzpeh before I did. Four words. He had read them three times.

That story: five points.

The other twenty-six points: sorting improvements, UI refinements, two API endpoints that fed a feature still three sprints away, a performance optimization that reduced load time by two hundred milliseconds on a screen that Dina had told him, when he showed her the prototype, she never looked at.

Twenty-six points of motion.

Five points of — he searched for the word — contact. Actual contact with the problem. The work that had touched Ronit's Tuesday and changed it.

He thought about Tamar. The conversation she'd had with Michal, which Michal had summarized for the team in the retrospective — not the full conversation, but the shape of it. The question Tamar had asked in her first sprint planning, quietly, after the third story was walked through: who decided the acceptance criteria for this?

The room had gone slightly still.

Lior and I shaped it in a refinement session on Tuesday, Michal had said.

Was Tamar in that session? Tamar had asked. Referring to herself in the third person with a lightness that took the edge off the directness.

No, Michal had said. I'll fix that.

It had been a small moment. The conversation had moved on. But Lior had noticed that the next two stories in the planning session had been walked through differently — Tamar asking questions, the team answering, the acceptance criteria emerging from the conversation rather than arriving pre-formed. It had taken longer. The stories had been better.

He closed the board.

Opened a message to Tamar.

I want to talk about the sprint format. I think it might be the wrong shape for where we are.

She came to his desk twenty minutes later, which he appreciated. Not a meeting, not a calendar invite. Just — I'm here, talk.

"The sprint," he said. "Two weeks. Time-boxed. Planning Monday, review Friday. We've been running it for four months."

"I know."

"In the beginning it made sense. The work was ambiguous. We needed the time-box to force decisions. Planning forced us to commit to something and the commitment was valuable because without it we'd have drifted."

"Right."

"But now —" He looked at the board. "The upstream work is cleaner. Tamar is shaping stories properly. The acceptance criteria are real. The domain model is cleaner since the EventStorming session. When a story arrives at the board now it arrives ready." He paused. "The sprint isn't doing the work it was doing before. It's become a container that we fill because we've always filled it."

Tamar looked at the board.

"What happens if you remove the container?" she said.

"The work becomes the unit. Not the sprint — the story. A story is ready, a developer pulls it. A story is done, they pull the next one. No waiting for sprint boundaries. No batch planning — continuous refinement instead, small sessions, stories shaped as they're needed rather than two weeks in advance."

"Kanban," she said.

"Yes."

"You know the objections."

"Tell me them."

She sat down. "First: predictability. The sprint gives the client a rhythm. They know what to expect on Friday. They know the demo is coming. Remove the sprint and you remove the client's window."

"The client doesn't actually attend the sprint review," Lior said. "Avi does. And Avi's window is the deployment — when something ships to the pilot clinics. That happens mid-sprint anyway, whenever the story is done. The sprint review is a ceremony that tells him what happened, not the moment it happens."

She nodded slowly. "Second objection: team coordination. Without the sprint boundary, how does the team stay synchronized?"

"Daily standup still exists. The board is visible. Blockers surface immediately because there's no sprint boundary to hide behind — a blocked story is just a blocked story, it doesn't get absorbed into sprint failure." He paused. "And the upstream rhythm is separate. Tamar's refinement sessions, the discovery work — that has its own cadence. Downstream flow doesn't need to be time-boxed for upstream to be disciplined."

"Third: accountability. The sprint commitment gives the team something to be accountable to."

"The WIP limits do that. One story per developer in progress. Finish before starting. The accountability is to the work, not to the time-box." He stopped. "The sprint commitment has been giving us something to be accountable to that's the wrong thing — points and velocity, not value delivered. Thirty-one points this sprint. Five of them touched something real."

Tamar was quiet.

"You've been thinking about this for a while," she said.

"Since Shira asked how we knew when something was done," he said. "The sprint answer is: when the sprint ends and the points are counted. The real answer is: when Ronit's Tuesday is better. Those are different clocks."

A long pause.

"Michal won't love this," Tamar said.

"Michal is changing faster than any of us," Lior said. "I think she'll hear it."

"Avi?"

"Avi cares about the pilot working. If the pilot clinics are getting improvements faster because we're shipping continuously instead of in two-week batches, he'll see that before he cares about the format."

Tamar looked at the board for a long time.

"The upstream work stays structured," she said. "My refinement sessions, the discovery cycles, the DoR — none of that changes. The stories still have to be ready before they're pulled."

"Yes. The upstream discipline is what makes the downstream flow possible. Without it, Kanban is just chaos with a board."

"And we measure cycle time. From pulled to deployed. Not velocity."

"Yes."

"And when the cycle time degrades — when stories start taking longer than they should — that's the signal that something is wrong upstream, not that the team needs to work harder."

"Exactly."

She stood.

"Let me talk to Michal," she said. "And Avi. If they're both in, we try it next sprint — or rather, instead of next sprint."

She paused at the edge of his desk.

"Lior. The morning prep screen. The five points." She looked at him. "That's not a coincidence. That story was the most thoroughly understood story on the board. Ronit's language in the acceptance criteria. Shira's edge cases written before you started. Yoav's —" She stopped. "Wait. Yoav hasn't started yet."

"Next week," Lior said.

"The screen has no design."

"It has Oren's implementation and Ronit's words. It works."

"It won't scale."

"No," he said. "That's why Yoav's coming."
