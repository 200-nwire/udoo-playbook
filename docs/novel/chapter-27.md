# Chapter 27 — What Carmit Said

<span class="part-label">Part Three — Onstream</span>

She read the postmortem on a Friday morning.

Avi had sent it with a covering note that he had written four times before settling on something that was, in the end, very short:

> We had an incident. Here is what happened, what we learned, and what we changed. I thought you should see the whole thing.

She read it in the coffee shop she used as a second office, with the careful attention she gave to documents that mattered. All the way through. The timeline, the contributing factors, the system analysis, the action items with their owners and dates.

When she finished she sat for a moment with her coffee.

Then she forwarded it to her head of IT with a one-line note:

> This is what good looks like.

Then she called Avi.

— I've been buying software for twenty years, she said. I have never received a postmortem from a vendor.

— We thought you should know, Avi said.

— Most vendors would have called it a service disruption and sent a resolution notice.

— It was a service disruption, Avi said. The resolution notice felt insufficient.

A silence.

— The action items, she said. The on-call rotation — is it running?

— Since Wednesday.

— The staging mirror?

— We have your sign-off on the data anonymization — we'll have it running by next Tuesday.

— The SLA consultation protocol?

— Dani drafted it yesterday. It's in the team process document.

She was quiet for a moment.

— Avi, she said. When we signed the pilot agreement, I told my board we were taking a risk on a small vendor. I told them the product was promising but unproven and that the real test would be how the team responded to problems, not whether problems occurred. She paused. — I want you to know that this — She stopped. — This is how I answer the question my board asked.

Avi sat with that for a moment.

— Thank you, he said.

— Don't thank me, she said. Just keep doing it.
