# Chapter Three — The Postmortem That Wasn't

<span class="part-label">Part One — Upstream</span>

They called it a lessons-learned session, which Eran had noticed was what you called a postmortem when you wanted it to feel less like an autopsy.

He was back at work. Twelve days after the hospital, pale in a way that still made people glance at him and then look away, as though illness were a thing you could catch through eye contact. His desk was the same. The coffee machine was the same. The particular quality of light through the office window at ten in the morning — the way it caught the dust in the air, made it visible for a few minutes before the angle changed — was the same.

Everything was the same and he was different and he was not sure yet what to do with that.

The lessons-learned session had four attendees: Eran, Dani, Yossi, and a woman named Shira from the management team who was there, as far as Eran could tell, to ensure that the lessons remained abstract.

Dani ran it. He had a slide. One slide, which Eran found both impressive and heartbreaking — that the entire collapse of the Strato production environment, the lost client hours, the three days of chaos, had been distilled into one slide with four bullet points.

> Key Learnings:
>
> Documentation should be kept up to date
> Deploy processes should be shared across the team
> Communication during incidents can be improved
> Single points of failure should be identified and mitigated

Eran read the slide. Read it again.

These were not wrong. They were, in the way of all lessons-learned bullet points, completely correct and completely useless — true at a level of generality so high that they could have been written before the incident, applied to any incident, and would have prevented nothing.

Documentation should be kept up to date. Yes. And people should exercise more and sleep eight hours and call their mothers.

"Eran," Dani said, "you want to walk us through what happened technically?"

He did. He walked them through the environment variable, the April Thursday, the payment gateway call, the number 8000. He explained it carefully, watching their faces — Dani taking notes, Yossi nodding with the focus of someone building a mental model, Shira writing something that Eran suspected was unrelated.

When he finished, Dani said: "So the action item is — you'll document the environment configuration?"

"Yes," Eran said. "But —"

He stopped.

But was the beginning of the sentence he had been rehearsing for twelve days. In the hospital, in the slow textured time of the drip and the ceiling and the forty-three Slack messages. He had thought about it the way you think about something when you have nothing to do but think — from every angle, setting it down and picking it up, wearing it smooth.

The sentence was: *But the problem isn't that I didn't document it. The problem is that we built a system where one person knowing something feels the same as the team knowing it. And we don't see that as a problem until the person isn't there.*

He looked at Shira's pen moving across her notebook.

He looked at the slide. Single points of failure should be identified and mitigated. There it was, already. Already a bullet point. Already contained.

"Nothing," he said. "Yes. I'll document it."

Yossi caught his eye across the table. Just for a moment — a look that was not quite a question, not quite an acknowledgment, but something in between. The look of someone who heard the but that didn't arrive and knew there had been one.

After, in the corridor, Yossi fell into step beside him.

"You were going to say something."

"Documentation action item," Eran said.

"Eran."

They walked. The corridor was long, fluorescent, the kind of space that flattens things.

"What's the point?" Eran said finally. Not bitterly. Just — genuinely. Asking.

Yossi thought about this seriously, which was something Eran had always respected about him. He didn't answer fast.

"I don't know," he said. "But I think — the way Dani runs those sessions. It's not that he doesn't care. He cares a lot. He's just —" He searched for it. "He's measuring something different. He needs the slide to be clean. He needs to go back to the client and say we ran a lessons-learned and here are the actions. The slide is the point."

"And the actual problem."

"The actual problem makes the slide messy."

They had reached the kitchen. Someone had left a mug on the counter with the specific confidence of a person who has never once washed up after themselves.

"You know what I keep thinking about?" Eran said. He poured water, watched it run. "I keep thinking about Lior. On the Kelev project."

"What about him?"

"He knows. He already knows. You can see it — the way he asks questions in standups, the way he —" He stopped. "He's going to leave."

"Probably," Yossi said.

"And then he'll be on some other project and in eight months he'll be the person who knows how the thing works. And one day he'll be sick, or he'll leave, and it'll happen again."

Yossi leaned against the counter. Outside the window, a delivery driver was having an argument with a parking meter with the committed energy of someone who expects to win.

"So what do you do with that?" Yossi said.

Eran didn't answer. Not because he didn't have one but because the answer he was forming was strange and new and he didn't yet trust it enough to say aloud. It had something to do with the difference between knowing and building knowledge. Something to do with the fact that he had spent seven years becoming the person who knew things, and had never once asked whether the knowing should live somewhere else.

"I don't know yet," he said.

Which was, for the first time in a long time, the truest thing he'd said at work.
