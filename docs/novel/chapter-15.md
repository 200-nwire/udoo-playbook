# Chapter 15 — Dani's Spreadsheet

The spreadsheet had 847 rows.

Dani knew this without counting — he had built it row by row over seventeen years, each project adding its sediment, the accumulated geology of a career spent tracking things. It had begun as a simple project tracker in Excel, had migrated through three software generations, had been rebuilt twice from scratch and each time had grown back to roughly the same shape, which told him something about the shape of the problem it was solving.

He opened it now, on a Friday evening, in the specific quiet of an office after everyone else had left — the quality of silence that is different from other silences, charged with the residue of the day's activity, the chairs slightly wrong, the coffee machine cooling.

The Kelev project: a tab. He clicked it.

He looked at it the way he looked at all of them — not reading it, exactly, but feeling its shape. The columns he had built and refined over years. Task. Owner. Status. Due Date. Hours Logged. Hours Remaining. Percent Complete. RAG Status. Red, Amber, Green. The traffic light system that clients understood immediately and that told them, at a glance, whether to worry.

The RAG status for Kelev: Amber.

He had changed it from Green to Amber on Wednesday, after the call with Michal in which she had told him about the debt register, the timezone assumption, the forty-one bugs. He had listened carefully, had asked precise questions, had made notes in his own shorthand that nobody else could read, and at the end had said: Thank you for telling me. I'll update the status and prepare a revised timeline for Avi.

Michal had been quiet for a moment.

Dani, she had said. The timeline isn't the problem.

He had understood what she meant. He had also not known what to do with it.

He had been a project manager for seventeen years because he was good at it, and he was good at it because he had learned, early, the thing that most people in client-facing roles learned eventually: that clients did not primarily want good news. They wanted certainty. They would accept bad news delivered with certainty more readily than good news delivered with ambiguity. What they could not tolerate — what made them reach for the phone, what made them escalate, what made them send the email that CCed three people — was not knowing.

The spreadsheet was his answer to not knowing.

Not his answer to the underlying problems. His answer to the client's experience of not knowing. A distinction he had understood for years and had not, until recently, examined.

He scrolled to the bottom of the Kelev tab.

Below the standard columns, below the RAG status, below the revised timeline he had prepared and sent to Avi on Wednesday evening, there was a row he had added on Thursday morning, before anyone arrived.

It wasn't a formula. It had no conditional formatting. It didn't feed into any summary dashboard.

It said: What did we learn this week about what the product actually needs to do?

Below it, in the adjacent cell, he had written: Clinic day has three distinct phases with different information needs. We were building for one. The briefing engine addresses morning prep. Scheduling intelligence needs discovery. Sigal's emergency triage problem is real and unaddressed.

He read it back.

It was, he recognized, a different kind of information than anything else in the spreadsheet. It was not a status. It was not a percentage. It could not be RAG-coded. There was no due date to attach to it. It described the shape of the problem, which was not a thing his spreadsheet had ever been designed to hold.

He thought about the lessons-learned session after the Strato incident. The slide with four bullet points. The but that Eran hadn't said.

He thought: I made that room safe for the bullet points and unsafe for the but. I did it deliberately, because the but would have made the slide messy and the slide had to go to the steering committee and the steering committee needed clean.

He thought: and the but was the thing that mattered.

He had known this, on some level, in the lessons-learned session. Had felt Eran's unsaid sentence the way you feel a change in air pressure — not consciously, but in the body, before the mind has named it. Had moved the conversation forward anyway because moving the conversation forward was his job and the job had, over seventeen years, become so thoroughly his identity that he had stopped being able to see where his judgment ended and his role began.

He opened a new document. Not the spreadsheet. Something else.

He wrote: The things I have been making it safe to say.

Then: The things I have been making it unsafe to say.

He sat with the second list for a long time. It was longer than the first, and more specific, and each item on it was something he recognized — a conversation he had redirected, a question he had absorbed and translated into something more manageable before it reached the people who needed to hear it, a concern he had logged privately and not escalated because escalation would have disturbed the motion and the motion was what he was paid to maintain.

He was not a dishonest person. He had never falsified a number, never told a client something he knew to be false, never hidden a crisis that needed to be visible. But there was a category of truth that was not a lie and was not the whole truth — the truth that was accurate at the level of the metric and insufficient at the level of what the metric was supposed to represent. Eighty-three percent complete. Progressing well. On track with revised timeline.

All true. All insufficient.

He thought about Carmit. The way she had said what's the slice and waited for an answer that came from understanding, not from a spreadsheet. The way she had looked at Eran when he said you've done this before. The way she had almost smiled.

He thought: she knows. She has been in client services for long enough that she knows the difference between a project manager and someone who understands the project.

He thought: I have been the first kind for seventeen years. I am not sure I know how to be the second kind.

He looked at the new row in the spreadsheet.

What did we learn this week about what the product actually needs to do?

It was a small thing. One row. The spreadsheet had 847 rows and this one didn't connect to any of them.

But it was the only row he had written in seventeen years that asked a question instead of recording an answer.

He saved the file.

Turned off his monitor.

Sat in the dark office for a moment longer than he needed to, in the specific quiet of a person who has just seen something they can't un-see and is deciding, not yet consciously but in the deeper place where real decisions are made, what to do with it.

Then he picked up his bag and went home.
