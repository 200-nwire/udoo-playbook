# Chapter 16 — The Product Owner

Her name was Tamar and she had been a product owner for three years at a company that did it properly, which meant she arrived with fluency in a language that the Kelev project didn't yet speak and had been trying, for two weeks, to determine whether this was her problem or theirs.

She had a certification. Not because she believed in certifications particularly but because her previous company had required it and she had done it and found, somewhat to her surprise, that the framework had a coherence she respected even where she disagreed with its specifics. She knew what a sprint review was for. She knew the difference between a product backlog and a sprint backlog. She knew that the product owner's job was to maximize value, which was the kind of sentence that was true and useless in equal measure until you were in a specific room with specific people and had to decide what it meant today.

She had read the Kelev initiative brief on her first day. Had read the Confluence pages — all of them, including the ones that contradicted each other. Had sat in on two standups and one sprint planning and had formed, quietly, a hypothesis that she had not yet shared with anyone.

The hypothesis was: this team is doing Scrum the way someone does a language they learned from a textbook. Grammatically correct. Communicatively incomplete.

She had been waiting for the right moment to say something like this. She had learned, from three years and one certification and more retrospectives than she could count, that the right moment was rarely the first week and almost never a standup.

She found Michal in the kitchen on a Tuesday morning, both of them waiting for the coffee machine with the companionable patience of people who have not yet decided whether they are allies.

"Can I ask you something?" Tamar said.

"Yes," Michal said, in the tone that meant I was hoping you would.

They took their coffees to the small meeting room. The one with the window that looked onto the courtyard, which had the particular quality of a space where real conversations happened because it was too small for performances.

"The sprint planning," Tamar said. "Last week. The stories came in with acceptance criteria already written. Estimates already on them. The team spent forty minutes — I timed it — reviewing things that had already been decided."

"Yes," Michal said carefully.

"Who decided them?"

"Lior mostly. Sometimes me, for the scope questions."

"Before the planning session."

"Before the planning session."

Tamar wrapped her hands around her coffee. "So the planning session was a —"

"Ratification," Michal said. The word had a specific weight to it, as though she had been holding it for a while.

"Right." Tamar looked out the window. Someone was crossing the courtyard below with the focused energy of a person going somewhere specific. "At my last company the planning session was where the team shaped the stories. The PO brought the problem — the what and why. The team figured out the how. The estimates came from the people doing the work, in the room, after they understood the work."

"That's how it's supposed to work," Michal said.

"Is that how it works here?"

A silence.

"It's how it worked before things got complicated," Michal said. "We were moving fast and the stories needed to be ready and —" She stopped. "I think we solved a coordination problem and created a different problem."

"The team doesn't own the stories."

"No."

"They execute them."

"Yes."

Tamar looked at her. "That's not a product team. That's a production line."

The words landed in the room with the clean impact of something true said without cruelty.

Michal felt two things simultaneously — the recognition that had become familiar to her lately, the feeling of a frame shifting — and underneath it something older and less comfortable. A defensiveness she examined quickly, found legitimate, and then set aside.

"I know," she said. "I've been watching it happen for weeks and I didn't have the language for it until now."

"What changed?"

"Several things." She thought about the Prague notebook. The diagnostic versus therapy distinction. The document she had written about what she had been managing around. "I think I confused maintaining the conditions for good work with doing the work for people."

Tamar was quiet for a moment, turning this over.

"There's a version of the PM role," she said slowly, "that's about protecting the team from uncertainty. Shaping everything before it arrives, so the developers never have to hold ambiguity. It feels like care. It looks like efficiency." She paused. "But it means the team never develops the muscle for sitting with uncertainty together. And then when uncertainty arrives anyway — which it always does — they don't know how to hold it."

Michal thought about the sprint where the data model problem had surfaced. The team's response — not panic, exactly, but a reaching-for-Michal, a waiting-for-someone-to-tell-them-what-to-do, that she had responded to by doing exactly that.

"I've been the person who holds the uncertainty," she said.

"Yes."

"So the team never had to."

"Yes."

A silence that was not uncomfortable. Two people looking at the same thing from different angles, finding that their views were consistent.

"So," Michal said. "What does the PO role actually look like here? Because I want to be honest with you — I'm not entirely sure where you end and I begin."

Tamar almost smiled. "That's the most useful thing anyone has said to me since I started."

They talked for an hour.

What emerged was not a clean org chart. It was messier and more honest than that — a map of the actual work, drawn from both sides, each of them naming what they did and what they wished someone else did and what was currently falling between them.

Michal's territory, as it had evolved: client relationship, timeline, stakeholder communication, ceremony facilitation, the space between the team and the outside world. She was good at this. It was real and necessary work.

Tamar's territory, as she understood it from her training and her previous role: the product backlog, the story shaping, the prioritization, the voice of the user in every sprint. The what and why that preceded every how.

The gap between them: discovery. The work of understanding the problem before shaping the solution. At Tamar's previous company this had lived clearly with the PO. Here it had been distributed and therefore owned by no one — Lior doing some of it in Ramat Gan, Avi doing some of it in wireframes on Sunday evenings, Michal doing some of it in the conversations she had with the client that never quite made it back to the team in their original form.

"Discovery is upstream of everything," Tamar said. "If the stories arrive at planning without it, you're not planning — you're guessing in an organized way."

"Lior's been doing some of it."

"I know. I read his document about Dina's Tuesday." She paused. "It's the best piece of product work on this project. And he's a developer."

"Is that a problem?"

"It's not a problem. It's a signal. When the developers are doing the best product thinking, it either means the developers are exceptional —" She glanced at Michal. "Which Lior is. Or it means there's a gap in the product function that gravity is pulling toward whoever is closest."

Michal thought about this. The gravity metaphor. Work falling toward available capacity rather than correct ownership.

"Both, probably," she said.

"Both," Tamar agreed. "So. If we're being explicit about ownership —"

She opened her laptop. Created a new document. Typed at the top: Who owns what on Kelev.

"Discovery and problem framing — that's mine. I need to be in the clinic with Lior next time. I need to understand Ronit and Sigal the way he does."

"Agreed."

"Story shaping — mine, collaboratively. I bring the problem. The team shapes the solution. Planning is a conversation, not a ratification."

"Agreed."

"Prioritization — mine, with input from you on client commitments and from Avi on strategy." She paused. "When those conflict, we have an explicit conversation rather than one of us quietly overriding the other."

Michal looked at her. "Has that been happening?"

"I've only been here two weeks," Tamar said carefully. "But I've seen two stories reprioritized mid-sprint based on client conversations that didn't come through me."

A silence.

"Yes," Michal said. "That's happened. I'll stop doing it."

"I need to know when client conversations have product implications. Same day."

"Yes."

"Stakeholder management, timelines, client relationship — yours. You're better at it than I would be and it's not the most valuable use of my time."

"Agreed."

"The ceremonies —" Tamar hesitated. "I want to run sprint planning and backlog refinement. You can stay in the room. But I need to run them, because the dynamic changes when the person with product ownership is facilitating."

This one cost Michal something. She felt it clearly — the small contraction of a person being asked to give up something they'd built. Not power exactly. Something more like identity. The retrospectives, the planning sessions, the standups — they had been the structure of her presence on the project, the visible form of her contribution.

She looked at the document on Tamar's screen.

Thought about the Prague notebook. Almost no team knows which they need at any given moment.

"Okay," she said. "Yes."

"You'll still facilitate retrospectives," Tamar said. "Because retrospectives are about the team's health, not the product direction. That's yours."

"And the diagnostic framing?"

Tamar looked at her. "Tell me about the diagnostic framing."

Michal told her about Prague. The German woman. The notebook. The difference between therapy and diagnosis.

Tamar listened without interrupting.

"I want to sit in on the next retrospective," she said when Michal finished. "Not to run it. To watch how you do it when you're doing it as diagnosis."

Michal felt something she hadn't expected to feel in this conversation.

Respect. Not the managed, professional kind. The real kind — the kind that arrives when someone takes your thinking seriously rather than your role.

"I'll warn you," she said. "I've only done it once. I'm still figuring out what it means."

"Good," Tamar said. "That's exactly the stage I want to watch."
