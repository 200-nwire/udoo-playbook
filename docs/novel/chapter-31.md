# Chapter 31 — Michal's Portfolio

She had been waiting for the conversation for three months.

Not anxiously. With the patience of someone who has learned — through the kind of experience that only comes from being wrong in consequential ways — that the right conversation arrives when the conditions for it are ready, and that forcing it early produces the form of the conversation without the substance.

The conditions were ready now.

She had watched Tamar take the product ownership. Had felt the initial contraction and then, more slowly, the opening — the specific relief of a person who has been carrying weight that wasn't entirely theirs and has found someone else who could hold their share of it. She was good at facilitation. She had always been good at it. But she had been using it to compensate for an absence — the absence of a clear product voice — and now that the absence was filled she could feel the difference between using a skill to compensate and using a skill for its actual purpose.

She sat with Avi on a Friday afternoon.

Not in the small room. In the office after most people had left, the particular quiet of late Friday, the coffee machine cooling, the city outside moving into the weekend.

I want to talk about what I'm doing, she said.

Avi looked at her. He had the quality, she had noticed, of someone who heard the thing behind the thing. It had taken her a while to trust this and she trusted it now.

Tell me, he said.

I've been a project manager, she said. On this project and the others before it. I've been good at it — I think I've been genuinely good at it — but I've been practicing it in a way that was partly compensating for things that should have existed and didn't. Discovery that nobody was doing. Product ownership that was distributed and therefore owned by everyone and no one. She paused. Tamar has the product function. Tamir has the infrastructure function. The team has learned to run itself in ways it couldn't before. Another pause. I'm not sure what I have.

Avi waited.

I think I have the portfolio, she said.

The word landed in the room and she felt, saying it, that it was right. Not as an aspiration but as a description of what she had actually been doing, inchoately, for months — watching the Kelev project and thinking about the next one, thinking about how the things learned here would transfer, thinking about the client relationships and the resourcing and the dependencies between projects that lived above the level of any single team.

We have three active projects, she said. Kelev, the Harutz project starting next month, and whatever we take on with the Vetcare full rollout. She opened her laptop. Not a slide — a document, plain text, the kind she had learned to use instead of presentations for conversations she wanted to be real. Each one has dependencies on the same pool of people. Tamir's infrastructure work serves all three. Shira's QA capacity is shared. Eran is nominally on Strato but functionally on Kelev. She looked at Avi. Nobody is managing those dependencies explicitly. I've been watching them informally, which is why I know about them. But informal knowledge—

—is the same problem as Eran's Confluence page, Avi said.

Yes.

He looked at her document.

Portfolio management, he said.

Yes. The layer above the individual projects. Who is working on what. Where the dependencies are. Where the risks compound across projects rather than inside them. Where the learnings from Kelev should inform how we start Harutz. She paused. And how we don't repeat what happened to Strato.

Avi was quiet for a moment.

Dani, he said.

Dani is changing, she said carefully. He's changing faster than I expected and I think he'll get there. But right now he's still primarily a project-level person. The portfolio layer is — She stopped. It's something I want to build. With your agreement.

What does it look like?

A monthly portfolio review, she said. All project leads, all resource owners, one room. Cross-project dependencies made visible. Learnings from each project offered to the others — not as mandates, as offers. And a process for how we start new projects that doesn't repeat the mistakes of the first weeks of Kelev.

Which were?

Discovery skipped. Roles assumed rather than defined. Infrastructure as an afterthought. QA as a checkpoint rather than a practice. She paused. We have the playbook now — not written down, distributed in people's heads. I want to write it down and make it the way we start every project.

Avi looked at her for a long time.

You've been thinking about this for a while, he said.

Three months, she said. Since the Tamar conversation.

Why didn't you say something sooner?

She thought about this honestly.

Because I wasn't sure it was mine to say, she said. Tamar had just arrived. Lior was finding something. The team was in the middle of changing. I didn't want to add another change before the current ones had landed. She paused. And because — honestly — I needed to understand my own role before I could articulate a different one. I couldn't have said this three months ago because I didn't know it yet.

Avi nodded slowly.

The portfolio review, he said. Start next month. I'll be there.

You don't need to be there every month, she said. You need to be there until it's running on its own.

He looked at her.

That's the most senior thing you've said to me, he said.

She felt the warmth of it without letting it distract her.

There's one more thing, she said. The way we onboard new projects. The discovery process — the stations, the domain workshops, the story mapping — I want that to be how we start Harutz. Not improvised. Designed. With Tamar and Lior involved from day one.

Tamar is on Kelev.

I know. But the first two weeks of Harutz will determine the next six months of it. I want to borrow her for those two weeks and build the foundation properly. She paused. The cost of doing it wrong is six months of building the wrong thing. The cost of doing it right is two weeks of Tamar's time.

Avi picked up his coffee. Cold by now. Drank it anyway, the way people drink cold coffee when they're thinking.

Yes, he said. Do it.

She closed her laptop.

Outside the window the city was Friday afternoon — the particular loosening of it, the change in the quality of motion as people moved toward the weekend. A man was walking a dog that was in no hurry. A couple was sitting on the steps of a building with food from somewhere, eating with the unhurried quality of people who had nowhere to be.

Michal, Avi said.

She looked at him.

The project management — the way you held things together when they had no business holding together. I want you to know I saw that. Even when it was compensating for things that should have existed. It was real work. It mattered.

She felt the specific quality of being seen by someone who had reason to see you — not as politeness but as accuracy.

Thank you, she said.

Now build the portfolio, he said.
