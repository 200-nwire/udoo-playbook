# Chapter Two — The Standup

<span class="part-label">Part One — Upstream</span>

The call started at nine.

It started at nine every morning, had started at nine every morning for the eleven weeks of the Kelev project, and in that time had developed the specific texture of a ritual that everyone performs and no one believes in — like a prayer said too many times, the words still correct, the meaning somewhere further back than anyone can currently reach.

Lior joined from his kitchen. Camera off. He always joined with camera off and had never been asked about it, which he took as either tolerance or indifference and had decided, for his own comfort, to call tolerance.

"Good morning everyone." This was Michal, the project manager. She had a quality Lior had spent weeks trying to name and had finally settled on relentless optimism — not the naive kind, but the professional kind, the kind that is maintained by effort and will and that you can see, if you look carefully, costs something. "Let's do a quick round. Lior, you want to start?"

He looked at the Jira board, open on his second monitor.

"Yesterday I was working on the dashboard component," he said. "Today I'll continue that. No blockers."

This was not entirely true. The blocker was the same as yesterday's blocker and the day before's blocker — that he didn't know what the dashboard was supposed to show, to whom, under what conditions — but he had learned, over eleven weeks, that naming this blocker in standup produced a specific sequence of events: Michal would say let me follow up on that, would follow up on it in a message to someone Lior couldn't see, and then nothing would happen, and the next morning he would say the same thing and the sequence would repeat. So he had stopped naming it. The blocker existed but had no path, and a blocker with no path is just the shape of the work.

"Great," Michal said. "Oren?"

Oren was the other developer. He joined every call from what appeared to be a very dark room, a single lamp behind him creating a chiaroscuro effect that made him look like a Rembrandt painting of someone who has been asked to implement a notification system without specifications.

"Same as yesterday," Oren said. "Working on the API integration."

A pause.

"Any progress?" Michal asked, with the careful brightness of someone asking a question they already know the answer to.

"Some," Oren said.

Another pause.

"Great," Michal said. "I have an update from the client side. They're very excited about the direction and they wanted me to pass on that they're really happy with the team's energy."

Lior looked at his coffee.

Energy. He thought about energy. He thought about what it meant that the client was measuring energy rather than — he searched for the word — outcomes. He thought about the dashboard component he had been working on for three days, which was technically progressing, in that lines of code were being written, and which was practically meaningless, in that he had no confirmed idea what it was supposed to do.

He thought: *someone told the client we have good energy and the client believed them and somewhere in that gap is the whole problem.*

He did not say this.

"Lior, one more thing." Michal's voice changed slightly, the brightness acquiring a faint undertone of something more careful. "I got a message from Avi asking about the timeline for the dashboard. He wants to show something to his investors next week."

Lior put down the coffee.

"Which dashboard?" he said.

A small silence.

"The... user dashboard. The one you're building."

"Right." He chose his words the way you choose footholds on uncertain ground. "I just want to make sure I'm building the right thing. The ticket says user should be able to see their data — I want to confirm which data, and what the user's context is when they open it. Like, are they checking in daily? Are they investigating a problem? That changes what should be prominent."

He could feel, through the silence, the shape of what he'd done — introduced a question where a status was expected, made the simple complicated, slowed the forward motion of a meeting that had been moving in a direction everyone had agreed, tacitly, not to examine too closely.

"That's a great question," Michal said, in the tone that means that is a question for another time. "Let me connect you with Avi directly and you can align."

"Great," Lior said.

"Great," Michal said.

The call ended. Nine-fourteen. Fourteen minutes exactly, the same as every morning. Lior stared at the screen that had returned to his desktop — a photo of Eilat from three summers ago, blue water, his sister laughing at something off-camera, a world that had no Jira board in it.

His phone buzzed.

<div class="chat-log">

**Michal**
Can we talk? Just 5 min

</div>

He typed: Sure.

Her face appeared immediately, as if she'd been waiting with her finger on the button.

"Hey." The relentless optimism was lower now, more human. "I just want to — look, I hear what you're saying about the dashboard. I do. But Avi is under a lot of pressure and if we start opening up scope questions now —"

"It's not a scope question," Lior said, as gently as he could. "I'm not asking for more time. I'm asking what I'm building."

Michal looked at him for a moment. She was maybe thirty-five, had been in project management for a decade, and had the eyes of someone who has sat in many rooms between the people who decide things and the people who build them and has learned, from experience, the cost of that position.

"I know," she said quietly. "I know you are."

Something in the way she said it made Lior stop.

"Avi gave me wireframes in week two," she continued. "I sent them to you. Did you —"

"The Figma link? Yes. It's six weeks old. The product changed."

"I know."

"Does Avi know the product changed?"

A pause that lasted a beat too long.

"He knows we're iterating," she said.

Lior looked at the Eilat photo. His sister laughing. Blue water.

"Michal," he said carefully. "What does Avi think we're building?"

She opened her mouth. Closed it. Looked somewhere off-camera — at a document, maybe, or a window, or the middle distance where difficult truths hover before they land.

"Something," she said finally, "that by the time we show him, he'll recognize."

The call ended.

Lior sat with that sentence for a long time.
