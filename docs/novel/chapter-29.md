# Chapter 29 — The OKR Conversation

<span class="part-label">Part Four — Offstream</span>

Avi called it a planning session.

Tamar called it, privately, a reckoning.

She had been preparing for it for two weeks — not the slides, she didn't have slides, she had a document, plain text, the kind of thing you write when you want to think rather than present. She had shared it with Lior and Michal the day before. Neither of them had suggested changes. Michal had written, in a comment at the bottom: *This is going to be uncomfortable. It's right.*

The room: Avi, Tamar, Michal, Lior, Dani, Netta. The small one, the courtyard window, Tuesday morning, the specific quality of light that arrived at nine and lasted until ten before the angle changed.

Avi opened with a slide.

One slide. Tamar looked at it and felt, simultaneously, respect for its clarity and apprehension about what it contained.

> Kelev — Q4 Objectives: 1. Complete Vetcare full rollout — all 42 clinics live by December 1. 2. Achieve 99.5% uptime across all production environments. 3. Reach 500 monthly active vets on the platform. 4. Reduce customer support tickets by 40%. 5. Launch scheduling intelligence feature — Sigal's problem.

He looked at the room.

— These are my objectives for the next quarter, he said. I want to know if they're the team's objectives.

A silence.

Tamar opened her document.

— I want to work through these one at a time, she said. Not to push back — to make sure we're measuring the same things.

Avi nodded.

— Number one, she said. Forty-two clinics by December first. That's a delivery objective. It tells us whether we shipped. It doesn't tell us whether what we shipped worked. She paused. What does success look like for the forty-two clinics? Not that they're live — that they're using it. That their Tuesdays are better.

— That's number three, Avi said. Five hundred monthly active vets.

— Monthly active is a leading indicator, Tamar said. It tells us vets are opening the app. It doesn't tell us whether Dina is still taking paper notes in the afternoon. She looked at him. We've been running the pilot for six weeks. We have one data point on actual behavior change — Netta's call with Dina. One clinic. We don't know what's happening in the other five.

— We're about to scale to forty-two, Lior said quietly. If something is wrong in the pilot, we're about to make it forty-two times more visible.

Avi looked at him.

— What are you suggesting?

— I'm suggesting that December first is a date, not an objective. The objective is forty-two clinics where vets have genuinely better Tuesdays. The date serves the objective. But if we hit December first with forty-two clinics live and Dina's problem in all of them — that's not success.

A silence.

Dani was writing in his notebook. Tamar could see, from her angle, that he was drawing something — not notes, a diagram. Two columns. She couldn't read the labels.

— The support ticket reduction, he said without looking up. Forty percent. That's a lagging indicator — it will tell us in ninety days whether we succeeded. What's the leading indicator? The thing we can measure now that predicts whether we'll hit forty percent in ninety days?

The room looked at him.

He looked up.

— I've been thinking about this, he said. Since the feedback loop conversation. Leading versus lagging. We've been setting objectives that are all lagging — they tell us afterward whether we succeeded. But by the time we know, it's too late to change course. He turned the notebook around. Two columns: Leading and Lagging. Against each of Avi's five objectives, he had written both. If we measure the leading indicators weekly, we know in week three whether we're on track for the lagging indicator in week twelve.

The room looked at the notebook.

Avi leaned forward.

— Where did this come from? he said.

Dani looked at him.

— The new column, he said. In my spreadsheet. A pause. I've been practicing.

---

They spent three hours on the objectives.

Not arguing — building. The process had the quality of the EventStorming session, of the story mapping, of the Three Amigos at their best — multiple perspectives finding, through friction, something more accurate than any one of them had arrived with.

By the end, Avi's five objectives had become something different. Not unrecognizable — the ambition was the same, the targets were roughly the same. But each one now had two measures: the lagging indicator that defined success, and the leading indicator that told you whether you were heading there.

Forty-two clinics live by December first became: forty-two clinics live by December first, with eighty percent of vets using the morning prep screen within their first week. The leading indicator: weekly active rate in pilot clinics — currently tracking at seventy-three percent, target eighty percent before full rollout.

Five hundred monthly active vets became: five hundred monthly active vets with average session length above three minutes. Below three minutes meant opening and closing without engaging. Above three minutes meant the app was doing something useful. The leading indicator: session length in the pilot, tracked weekly.

Reduce support tickets by forty percent became: reduce support tickets by forty percent, with zero tickets related to data accuracy. The data accuracy qualifier came from Netta, who had been tracking ticket categories and had found that the highest-frustration tickets — the ones that correlated with churn risk — were not usability issues but trust issues. Wrong data. Missing data. The balance display problem, in a different form, in a different product.

Scheduling intelligence stayed. But Tamar added a prerequisite: discovery sprint with Sigal before a line of code is written. Avi agreed immediately, which told Tamar something about how much the EventStorming session had changed him.

Ninety-nine point five uptime became: ninety-nine point five uptime, measured and reported transparently to all clients monthly. Tamir's contribution. Quietly said. The room had accepted it without discussion, which was itself a measure of how much had changed.

At the end, Avi looked at what they'd built.

— These are better, he said. They're harder to hit.

— They're harder to fake, Tamar said.

He looked at her.

— Yes, he said. That's what I meant.
