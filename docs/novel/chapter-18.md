# Chapter 18 — Yoav

<span class="part-label">Part Two — Downstream</span>

He arrived with a laptop, a drawing tablet, and the specific confidence of someone who has been told he is good at his job often enough and by enough different people that he has stopped questioning it and started, in certain moments, depending on it.

His portfolio was real. Lior had looked at it before the first call — twelve projects, each one photographed with the care of someone who understood that the presentation of work was itself a form of work. Clean interfaces. Considered typography. The kind of visual intelligence that makes you feel, looking at a screen, that whoever made this had thought carefully about you.

He had worked at a studio before this. The kind of place that won awards and published case studies and had a process that moved in one direction: client brief to research to wireframes to high-fidelity mockups to handoff. A linear flow, each stage completing before the next began, the designer's contribution arriving as a finished artifact rather than a developing conversation.

He had been good at that flow. He was about to discover that the flow was the problem.

His first week he observed.

This was Michal's suggestion and Tamar's insistence and he had agreed to it with the grace of someone who didn't fully believe in it but could see that the room did, and that the room's belief was worth respecting until he had evidence one way or the other.

He sat in on a refinement session. Watched Tamar walk through a story — not a finished story, a problem statement, rough, with questions still visible in it. Watched the team pull it apart. Watched Shira find the edge case in seven minutes that had taken Lior a full day to find in Ramat Gan. Watched Oren sketch something on the whiteboard that was not a wireframe and not an architecture diagram but something between them, a thinking-tool, quick and functional and disposable.

He sat in on Lior and Shira's version of the Three Amigos session — the term Tamar had introduced the week before, the three-way conversation between product, development, and quality before a story was started. He watched them talk about acceptance criteria the way he talked about design decisions — with the specific vocabulary of people who had learned to disagree productively, who had enough shared language that friction generated insight rather than heat.

He noticed that nobody had mentioned design.

Not as an absence — they weren't building UI-heavy features this week, mostly backend and data model work. But he had the feeling of someone watching a process that had found its rhythm without him, that had developed its own internal logic, and that would require a specific kind of entry — not an imposition but a joining.

On Thursday he asked Tamar: "When does design come in?"

She looked at him with the expression he was beginning to recognize as her thinking face — not delay, active processing.

"That's the question," she said. "What do you think?"

He had expected an answer, not a question. He sat with it.

"At the wireframe stage," he said. "Traditionally. Once the problem is understood and before the development starts."

"What's the problem with that?"

He thought about the Figma file. Avi's forty-seven screens, built from the outside, before Lior had gone to Ramat Gan, before anyone had watched Ronit work. Beautiful. Wrong.

"The problem," he said slowly, "is that by the wireframe stage someone has already made assumptions about the solution. And the wireframe encodes those assumptions. And then development builds to the wireframe. And the assumptions never get questioned because they're embedded in something that looks finished."

Tamar waited.

"So design should come in earlier," he said. "Before the wireframe. At the problem stage."

"Yes," she said. "And also later. At the implementation stage, checking whether what's been built matches what was intended. And at the testing stage, looking at what Shira finds and asking whether the design caused it."

"Design throughout," he said.

"Design throughout," she said. "Not as a phase. As a perspective."

He thought about twelve projects in his portfolio. Each one a phase. Each one delivered as an artifact. Each one, he now realized, a finished answer to a question that had been asked without him in the room.
