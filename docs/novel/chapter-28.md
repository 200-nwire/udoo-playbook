# Chapter 28 — Netta's Loop

<span class="part-label">Part Four — Offstream</span>

The clinic that hadn't called back called back on a Monday.

It was Dina. Not the clinic manager — Dina herself, who had apparently found Netta's message in the three-week archaeological layer of her voicemail and had called during a gap between patients, which meant the call had a specific texture — present but slightly distracted, the quality of attention of someone who is giving you what they have rather than what the situation ideally requires.

— Sorry for the delay, Dina said. Mondays.

— I know, Netta said. How's Tuesday?

A pause that had something in it.

— Better, Dina said. Mostly.

— Tell me the mostly.

Another pause. Netta had learned to sit in pauses rather than fill them. The pause was where the real information lived.

— The morning prep is good, Dina said. The Mitzpeh thing — knowing what I need to know before I go in. That's good. That's real. A beat. But there's a moment in the afternoon. Between two and three, when the morning appointments are done and the afternoon ones haven't started, and I need to write up my notes from the morning before I forget the details. The system — She stopped.

— The system, Netta said.

— It's slow. Not broken. Just slow. When I have five sets of notes to write and fifteen minutes to write them, the load time per record — it adds up. I've started taking paper notes in the morning and transcribing them in the afternoon, which is — a pause — exactly what I was doing before your system.

Netta wrote this down.

Not in a personal notebook. In the feedback log. The shared one, the one she had built with Tamar in her first week, the one that both CS and product could see.

— How long has this been happening?

— Since the beginning of the pilot.

— You didn't mention it before.

— No one asked, Dina said. Not with blame. Just — noting it.

— I'm asking now, Netta said.

— I know, Dina said. And something in her voice changed — not much, but perceptibly, the slight warmth of someone who has been enduring something in silence and has just been given permission to name it. The afternoon notes window. Two to three PM. That's when it matters most.

— I'll make sure the right people see this today, Netta said. Not next sprint. Today.

— Thank you, Dina said.

The call ended.

Netta looked at the feedback log entry she'd written.

> Source: Dina, HaRefu'ah clinic. Signal: record load time — slow in afternoon window. Impact: user reverting to paper notes for AM records, transcribing PM. Duration: since pilot start. Category: performance/UX friction. Revenue at risk: single clinic — but this is the reference clinic. If Dina is reverting to paper, what are the other five doing?

She flagged it. Tagged Tamar, Lior, Tamir.

Then she added one line at the bottom:

> This signal has been present since pilot start. It was not collected because no one was asking. Recommend: quarterly user calls for all pilot clinics as standing practice.

She sat back.

Thought about the six months at her previous company when she had been logging feedback into a Notion document that nobody read. The feeling of signals disappearing into a system that had no output. The gradual, demoralizing learning that logging things was a performance of usefulness rather than usefulness itself.

This was different.

She could already see Tamar's typing indicator in Slack.

<div class="chat-log">

**Tamar:** On it — pulling the performance logs for the afternoon window now. Lior — this might be the database query issue we flagged in the architecture session. Give me an hour.

</div>

She thought: *that's what a feedback loop feels like when it's working.*

Not the logging. The response. The signal entering the system and something moving in response to it, within the hour, while Dina was still seeing patients.

She opened a new entry in the log.

> Follow-up action: establish quarterly check-in calls for all pilot clinics. Owner: Netta. Target: before Vetcare full rollout.

She looked at it.

Added:

> Purpose — not to collect feature requests. To ask how Tuesday is. To hear the mostly.
