# Chapter Five — Alignment

They met on a Thursday, on a video call that Michal had arranged with the careful orchestration of someone seating guests at a wedding — Lior on one side, Avi on the other, herself in the middle, the agenda typed into the invite: Dashboard alignment — 30 min.

Lior had prepared. He had opened the Figma file — the old one, the Sunday evening one — and studied it the way he studied things when he was trying to understand the mind behind them rather than just the content. He had looked at the annotations. Vet sees today's appointments at a glance. Most urgent first. One tap to open record. He had looked at the flow from the dashboard to the patient record to the billing screen and back. He had looked at it and felt something he hadn't expected to feel.

It was good. It was genuinely, carefully good — the work of someone who understood the problem, who had sat with it and thought about it, who had drawn not what looked like a dashboard but what felt like a Tuesday morning for a vet with fourteen appointments and three urgent callbacks and the specific cognitive load of a person whose job is to hold many things in mind simultaneously.

He had looked at what he'd built. Then at the wireframe. Then at what he'd built.

He had opened a new file and started writing questions.

Now he was on the call, the questions on a notepad beside the keyboard, and Avi Shapiro was on the screen — older than Lior had imagined, with the kind of face that had earned its lines — and Michal was saying something about exciting opportunity to align and Lior was watching Avi's eyes and thinking: this man knows something is wrong and has been too polite to say so.

"Avi," Lior said, at the first pause. "Can I ask — when you made the wireframes. The dashboard. Can you tell me about the moment you were designing for?"

Michal's expression shifted almost imperceptibly.

Avi looked at Lior for a moment — directly, with an attention that felt different from the attention of the calls Lior usually attended, which was the divided attention of people doing three things while appearing to do one.

"Tuesday morning," Avi said. "Eight forty-five. The clinic opens at nine. The vet — her name is Ronit, she's been using paper records for twelve years — she has fourteen appointments, two of which were added last night after the system closed, one of which is an emergency that came in this morning. She has seven minutes before the first patient arrives." He paused. "She needs to know, in those seven minutes, what today is. Not everything. Just what today is."

Silence.

"The current build," Lior said carefully, "shows all appointments in chronological order. No prioritization. No flag for same-day additions. The emergency would be —" He checked. "Fourth in the list."

Avi nodded slowly. Not with anger. With the recognition of a man who has suspected something for weeks and is now watching the suspicion become specific.

"How long to change that?" he said.

And here was the moment. Lior felt it the way you feel a current underwater — invisible, present, pulling.

The easy answer was a number. Two days. Three days. A number that would make Michal's shoulders drop with relief and move the call toward great, let's align on next steps and preserve the shape of progress that everyone had agreed, without discussing it, to maintain.

He looked at his notepad.

"I don't know yet," he said. "Because I think the prioritization logic depends on how you define urgent. And I think that definition might be different for different clinics. And I think if we get it wrong we'll build something that Ronit ignores, and then the whole dashboard gets ignored, and the problem we started with — the paper records, the seven minutes — we haven't touched it."

Michal was very still.

Avi leaned forward slightly.

"Say more," he said.

And something happened then that Lior would think about for a long time afterward. Not a dramatic thing. Not a breakthrough or a revelation. Just a shift — the almost physical sensation of a conversation changing register. Of two people deciding, simultaneously and without discussing it, to stop performing progress and start looking at the thing itself.

"I have questions," Lior said. "They're probably things you know the answers to. But I think I need the answers before I write another line of code."

He picked up the notepad.

"First one: does Ronit have a tablet or a desktop at the clinic? Because the information hierarchy I'd design for a seven-inch screen is completely different from —"

"Tablet," Avi said immediately. "Mostly. Some clinics have desktop at reception."

"Okay." Lior wrote it. "Second: the emergency flag. Who sets it? Is it the system, based on some rule, or is it a human — the receptionist, maybe — who marks something as urgent?"

"Currently the receptionist. She writes it in a physical notebook."

"Okay." Wrote it. "Third — and this is the bigger one, and I should have asked it in week one, and I'm sorry I didn't — when Ronit opens the app on Tuesday morning. What does success look like? Not for the app. For her. What's the feeling at eight fifty-eight, before the first patient comes in, if everything has gone right?"

Avi was quiet for a moment.

Outside whatever window was behind him — Lior could see trees, the edge of a building, grey sky — something moved. A bird, maybe. A shadow.

"She feels like she knows what she's walking into," Avi said quietly. "That's all. She just — knows. Without having to look in three places. Without having to remember what she checked last night. She opens it and she knows."

Lior wrote: Knows. One place. Without effort.

Looked at what he'd built. The chronological list. The neutral interface. The dashboard that showed data but told nothing.

"Okay," he said. "I need three days. Not to build — to think, and to show you something small that we can look at together before I build the real thing. Is that —"

He glanced at Michal.

She was looking at him with an expression he couldn't quite read. Not anger. Not relief. Something more complicated — the expression of a person watching something happen that she had not planned for and does not yet know whether to call good or bad.

"That's fine," Avi said. "That's more than fine." A pause. "I should have had this conversation in week one."

"So should I," Lior said.

Michal opened her mouth.

Closed it.

Made a note.
