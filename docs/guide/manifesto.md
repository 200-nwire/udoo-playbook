# The Manifesto

We got into this work because we wanted to build things that matter.

Not just code that compiles. Not just features that ship. Things that solve a real problem for a real person — and leave us feeling like we did something worth doing.

But somewhere between the idea and the release, most of us have experienced the drift. The sprint where nobody is quite sure what "done" means. The spec that was clear until development began and then somehow wasn't. The feature that launched and was used by almost nobody. The incident that happened because the person who knew the answer wasn't on call that night. The customer who left quietly, and the discovery — months later — that we had signals we didn't act on.

We have all been there. Not because we aren't good enough. Because we didn't have a shared language for how to work together.

---

This framework exists because of that drift.

Not to add process for its own sake. Process for its own sake is what makes people leave teams. What we're after is something simpler and harder: a team where everyone knows what they're responsible for, where the right people are in the right conversations at the right time, where something finished actually means something finished.

A team where a developer doesn't spend half a sprint deciphering what the PM meant. Where a designer's work doesn't get rediscovered at code review. Where QA isn't asked to catch things that could have been named before the first keystroke. Where an on-call engineer has a runbook instead of a panic.

A team where, at the end of a release, everyone can honestly say: we built the right thing, we built it right, and we know it's running.

---

We wrote this playbook from the inside out — from the failures, not the ideals.

Every practice in here exists because a team ran into a wall without it. The Definition of Ready exists because stories without one destroy sprints. The Experience Snapshot exists because features without a human story at their center get built for imagined users, not real ones. The blameless post-mortem exists because blame is a dead end — it stops learning and starts hiding.

We believe that how a team works together is not secondary to what they build. It is the same thing. A team that discovers together, ships together, and learns together will build better software than a team with twice the talent and half the trust.

---

Here is what we're committed to:

**Clarity before code.** We name the problem before we name the solution. We name the user before we name the feature. We name the edge case before the developer finds it.

**Narrative before specification.** A story that a human can read aloud is worth more than a ticket a system can index. We write for the person we're building for, not for the tool that tracks the work.

**Finishing before starting.** A story that is 80% done delivers 0% of its value. We finish things. We pull when ready, not when eager.

**Gates protect the next person.** A Definition of Ready protects the developer. A Definition of Done protects the QA lead. A runbook protects the engineer on call at midnight. We write these things not because the framework says to, but because we care about the person who comes next.

**The loop never stops.** What we learn in production feeds what we build next. What customers tell CS shapes the next initiative. We treat Offstream as the beginning of something, not the end.

---

This is not a framework to follow. It is a way of working to practice — with your team, on your real project, starting from where you actually are.

You don't need to adopt everything at once. You need to adopt the thing that addresses your worst current pain, and hold to it until it becomes habit. Then the next thing. And the next.

The teams that use this well are not the ones who follow it most faithfully. They are the ones who understand it deeply enough to know when to adapt it — and why.

We hope you find it useful. More than that, we hope it helps your team become one where people are proud not just of what they ship, but of how they work together to ship it.

That is worth building for.
